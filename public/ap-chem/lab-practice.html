LAB 01 · ACID-BASE TITRATION

Titration & Equivalence Point

Recognizing the equivalence point on a titration curve and using volume data to find unknown concentration.

We Do
SHORT-ANSWER FRQ

A student titrates 25.00 mL of an unknown HCl solution with 0.100 M NaOH. The equivalence point is reached after adding 32.50 mL of NaOH.

(a) Write the balanced neutralization equation.
(b) Calculate the concentration of the HCl solution.
(c) What indicator would be appropriate, and why?

STEP 1 · Balanced equation
HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l)
1:1 mole ratio — that's the key.
STEP 2 · Moles of NaOH used
mol NaOH = M × V = 0.100 mol/L × 0.03250 L = 3.25 × 10⁻³ mol
STEP 3 · Apply the 1:1 ratio
At the equivalence point, mol acid = mol base (because the ratio is 1:1). mol HCl = 3.25 × 10⁻³ mol
STEP 4 · Solve for HCl concentration
[HCl] = mol / V = 3.25 × 10⁻³ mol / 0.02500 L [HCl] = 0.130 M
STEP 5 · Choose an indicator
Strong acid + strong base → equivalence pH = 7. Use an indicator that changes color near pH 7, like bromothymol blue (pH 6.0-7.6). Phenolphthalein (pH 8.2-10) also works because the steep pH jump at equivalence covers that range too.
ANSWER
(a) HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O
(b) [HCl] = 0.130 M
(c) Bromothymol blue, because it changes color near pH 7 (the equivalence pH for a strong acid + strong base).
You Do
SHORT-ANSWER FRQ

A student titrates 20.00 mL of an unknown H₂SO₄ solution with 0.150 M KOH. The equivalence point is reached after adding 28.40 mL of KOH.

(a) Write the balanced neutralization equation.
(b) Calculate the concentration of the H₂SO₄ solution.
(c) Watch out: this is not a 1:1 ratio.

Sample Response
(a) H₂SO₄ + 2 KOH → K₂SO₄ + 2 H₂O
Sulfuric acid is diprotic — needs 2 mol base per mol acid.

(b) Step 1 — moles of KOH: mol KOH = 0.150 × 0.02840 = 4.26 × 10⁻³ mol Step 2 — apply the 1:2 ratio (acid:base): mol H₂SO₄ = 4.26 × 10⁻³ × (1/2) = 2.13 × 10⁻³ mol Step 3 — concentration: [H₂SO₄] = 2.13 × 10⁻³ / 0.02000 = 0.107 M If you forgot to divide by 2, you'd get 0.213 M — exactly double the right answer. This is the most common error on diprotic acid titrations.
LAB 02 · SOLUTION PREPARATION

Dilution & M₁V₁ = M₂V₂

Preparing a target concentration from a stock solution — the workhorse of every wet lab.

We Do
MULTIPLE CHOICE

A student needs 250.0 mL of 0.200 M HCl. They have a stock solution of 6.00 M HCl.

What volume of stock solution should they use?

A.4.17 mL
B.8.33 mL
C.12.0 mL
D.30.0 mL
STEP 1 · Identify what each variable is
M₁V₁ = M₂V₂. Subscript 1 = stock (concentrated), subscript 2 = final (dilute).
M₁ = 6.00 M, V₁ = ? (what we want), M₂ = 0.200 M, V₂ = 250.0 mL
STEP 2 · Solve for V₁
V₁ = (M₂ × V₂) / M₁ V₁ = (0.200 × 250.0) / 6.00 = 8.33 mL
STEP 3 · Sanity check
Diluting from 6.00 M to 0.200 M is a 30× dilution. So we need 1/30 of the final volume of stock: 250 / 30 ≈ 8.3 mL. ✓
STEP 4 · How to actually do it in lab
Measure 8.33 mL of 6.00 M HCl with a pipette, add to a 250 mL volumetric flask, then fill to the 250 mL line with distilled water. Never add water to concentrated acid — always acid to water.
ANSWER
B. 8.33 mL

LAB 03 · GRAVIMETRIC ANALYSIS

Empirical Formula from Mass Data

Heating a hydrate to drive off water, then using mass loss to determine the formula. Classic AP exam material.

We Do
LONG-ANSWER FRQ

A 3.246 g sample of a hydrate of copper(II) sulfate, CuSO₄·xH₂O, is heated until all the water is driven off. The mass of the anhydrous CuSO₄ remaining is 2.076 g.

(a) Calculate the mass of water lost.
(b) Determine the value of x in the formula.
Molar masses: CuSO₄ = 159.6 g/mol, H₂O = 18.02 g/mol

STEP 1 · Mass of water lost
mass H₂O = 3.246 g − 2.076 g = 1.170 g
STEP 2 · Convert each to moles
mol CuSO₄ = 2.076 g / 159.6 g/mol = 0.01301 mol mol H₂O = 1.170 g / 18.02 g/mol = 0.06493 mol
STEP 3 · Find the mole ratio
Divide both by the smaller number (mol CuSO₄): CuSO₄: 0.01301 / 0.01301 = 1.00 H₂O: 0.06493 / 0.01301 = 4.99 ≈ 5
STEP 4 · Write the formula
x = 5, so the hydrate is CuSO₄·5H₂O (copper(II) sulfate pentahydrate — the bright blue crystals you've seen in lab).
ANSWER
(a) Mass of water lost = 1.170 g
(b) x = 5, formula is CuSO₄·5H₂O
You Do
LONG-ANSWER FRQ

A 2.500 g sample of a hydrate of magnesium sulfate, MgSO₄·xH₂O, is heated until all the water is driven off. The mass of anhydrous MgSO₄ remaining is 1.222 g.

(a) Calculate the mass of water lost.
(b) Determine the value of x in the formula.
Molar masses: MgSO₄ = 120.4 g/mol, H₂O = 18.02 g/mol

Sample Response
(a) mass H₂O = 2.500 g − 1.222 g = 1.278 g
(b) Step 1 — convert to moles: mol MgSO₄ = 1.222 / 120.4 = 0.01015 mol mol H₂O = 1.278 / 18.02 = 0.07093 mol Step 2 — ratio (divide by smaller): MgSO₄: 0.01015 / 0.01015 = 1.00 H₂O: 0.07093 / 0.01015 = 6.99 ≈ 7 Answer: x = 7, formula is MgSO₄·7H₂O (Epsom salts — same stuff you put in a bath).
LAB 04 · GAS COLLECTION OVER WATER

Dalton's Law & Molar Volume

When you collect a gas by water displacement, the gas in the tube is contaminated with water vapor. You need to subtract it out.

We Do
SHORT-ANSWER FRQ

A student reacts magnesium with hydrochloric acid and collects the H₂ gas by water displacement. The collected gas has a volume of 125.0 mL at 25 °C and the total pressure inside the tube is 752 torr. At 25 °C, the vapor pressure of water is 23.8 torr.

(a) Find the partial pressure of dry H₂.
(b) Calculate the moles of H₂ collected.
R = 0.0821 L·atm/(mol·K)

STEP 1 · Why we subtract water vapor
When gas is collected over water, the gas above the water is a mixture of the gas we want (H₂) and water vapor. By Dalton's Law: P_total = P_H₂ + P_H₂O.
STEP 2 · Solve for P of H₂
P_H₂ = P_total − P_H₂O = 752 − 23.8 = 728.2 torr Convert to atm: 728.2 / 760 = 0.9582 atm
STEP 3 · Convert units for PV=nRT
V = 125.0 mL = 0.1250 L
T = 25 °C = 298 K (always Kelvin!)
P = 0.9582 atm
STEP 4 · Solve for n
n = PV / RT n = (0.9582)(0.1250) / [(0.0821)(298)] n = 0.1198 / 24.47 = 4.90 × 10⁻³ mol H₂
ANSWER
(a) P_H₂ = 728.2 torr (or 0.9582 atm)
(b) n_H₂ = 4.90 × 10⁻³ mol
You Do
SHORT-ANSWER FRQ

A student reacts zinc with hydrochloric acid and collects the H₂ gas by water displacement. The collected gas has a volume of 85.0 mL at 22 °C and the total pressure inside the tube is 745 torr. At 22 °C, the vapor pressure of water is 19.8 torr.

(a) Find the partial pressure of dry H₂.
(b) Calculate the moles of H₂ collected.
R = 0.0821 L·atm/(mol·K)

Sample Response
(a) Dalton's Law: P_H₂ = 745 − 19.8 = 725.2 torr P_H₂ = 725.2 / 760 = 0.9542 atm (b) Set up PV = nRT: V = 0.0850 L, T = 22 + 273 = 295 K n = (0.9542)(0.0850) / [(0.0821)(295)] n = 0.08111 / 24.22 = 3.35 × 10⁻³ mol H₂ Common pitfall: Forgetting to subtract water vapor. If you used 745 torr directly, you'd get ~3.44 × 10⁻³ mol — close enough to look "right" but actually wrong. Always subtract.
LAB 05 · BEER-LAMBERT LAW

Calibration Curves & Concentration

Using a calibration curve to find an unknown concentration from absorbance — the most quantitative skill on the AP exam outside of titration.

We Do
SHORT-ANSWER FRQ

A student prepares standard solutions of Cu²⁺ and measures their absorbance at 620 nm.

[Cu²⁺] (M)Absorbance
0.0500.105
0.1000.215
0.2000.428
0.3000.640

An unknown Cu²⁺ sample has an absorbance of 0.345 under the same conditions.

(a) What is the relationship between absorbance and concentration here?
(b) Calculate the concentration of Cu²⁺ in the unknown.

STEP 1 · State Beer-Lambert Law
A = εbc
A = absorbance, ε = molar absorptivity (a constant for a given substance + wavelength), b = path length (cuvette width, constant), c = concentration.
If ε and b are constant, A is directly proportional to c — a straight line through the origin.
STEP 2 · Verify the data is linear
Check A/c for each row: 0.105 / 0.050 = 2.10 0.215 / 0.100 = 2.15 0.428 / 0.200 = 2.14 0.640 / 0.300 = 2.13 All roughly 2.13 — confirms linear behavior. The slope (εb) ≈ 2.13 M⁻¹.
STEP 3 · Find the best-fit slope
Use the average of the slopes (or do a linear regression): slope ≈ 2.13 M⁻¹ So the calibration equation is: A = 2.13 × c
STEP 4 · Solve for unknown
c = A / slope = 0.345 / 2.13 = 0.162 M
ANSWER
(a) Absorbance is directly proportional to concentration (Beer-Lambert Law, A = εbc).
(b) [Cu²⁺] in unknown ≈ 0.162 M

SHORT-ANSWER FRQ

A student prepares standard solutions of FeSCN²⁺ and measures their absorbance at 447 nm.

[FeSCN²⁺] (M)Absorbance
1.0 × 10⁻⁴0.180
2.0 × 10⁻⁴0.362
3.0 × 10⁻⁴0.540
4.0 × 10⁻⁴0.722

An unknown FeSCN²⁺ sample has an absorbance of 0.415.

(a) Determine the slope of the calibration curve.
(b) Calculate the concentration of FeSCN²⁺ in the unknown.

Sample Response
(a) Check ratios A/c: 0.180 / 1.0×10⁻⁴ = 1800 0.362 / 2.0×10⁻⁴ = 1810 0.540 / 3.0×10⁻⁴ = 1800 0.722 / 4.0×10⁻⁴ = 1805 All very close to 1800 — the data is linear. Slope ≈ 1800 M⁻¹ (this is εb).

(b) Use the slope: c = A / slope = 0.415 / 1800 = 2.31 × 10⁻⁴ M That falls between the 2.0 × 10⁻⁴ and 3.0 × 10⁻⁴ standards, which is exactly where it should be given an absorbance of 0.415 (between 0.362 and 0.540). ✓
LAB 06 · QUALITATIVE ANALYSIS

Precipitation, Filtration & Identifying Unknowns

Using solubility rules and selective precipitation to figure out what's in an unknown solution.

We Do
MULTIPLE CHOICE

A student has an unknown solution that contains one of these cations: Ag⁺, Ba²⁺, or Cu²⁺.

When dilute HCl is added, a white precipitate forms. Which cation is present?

A.Ag⁺
B.Ba²⁺
C.Cu²⁺
D.Cannot be determined
STEP 1 · What's the anion we just added?
HCl provides Cl⁻. So the precipitate has to be a chloride salt of whichever cation is in solution.
STEP 2 · Check solubility rules for chlorides
Most chlorides are soluble — the famous exceptions are AgCl, PbCl₂, and Hg₂Cl₂.
So among our three options: AgCl is insoluble (precipitates), BaCl₂ is soluble (no precipitate), CuCl₂ is soluble (no precipitate).
STEP 3 · Color check
A second clue: AgCl is white. Cu²⁺ solutions are blue, so a Cu precipitate would also be colored. Ba²⁺ wouldn't precipitate at all here. Everything points to Ag⁺.
STEP 4 · Confirm with the net ionic equation
Ag⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) → AgCl(s)
ANSWER
A. Ag⁺. The white precipitate is AgCl. Add NaCl or HCl to a silver solution and you get the classic curdy white precipitate that turns purple in light.
