Electron Configuration & PES

We Do
MULTIPLE CHOICE

Which of the following is the correct ground-state electron configuration for a neutral atom of Mn (Z = 25)?

A.[Ar] 4s² 3d⁵
B.[Ar] 3d⁵ 4s²
C.[Ar] 4s² 3d⁷
D.[Ne] 3s² 3p⁶ 4s² 3d⁵
STEP 1 · Locate Mn
Mn is element 25. So we need to place 25 electrons total.
STEP 2 · Use Ar as a noble gas core
Ar = 18 electrons. That leaves 25 − 18 = 7 electrons to place after [Ar].
STEP 3 · Fill in order of energy
After Ar, the next subshells fill 4s then 3d. Place 2 in 4s, then 5 in 3d.
STEP 4 · Write by shell, not by filling order
When we write the configuration, we list subshells by principal quantum number: 3d comes before 4s. So the correct written form is [Ar] 3d⁵ 4s². Choice (A) shows the filling order, which is why (B) is the answer AP wants.
ANSWER
B. [Ar] 3d⁵ 4s²

TOPIC 02 · UNIT 2

Bond Polarity & Molecular Shape

We Do
SHORT-ANSWER FRQ

(a) Predict the molecular geometry of CO₂.
(b) The C–O bonds are polar, but the molecule itself is nonpolar. Explain why.

STEP 1 · Draw the Lewis structure
Carbon has 4 valence electrons, each oxygen has 6. Total = 16. The structure is O=C=O with two double bonds and no lone pairs on C.
STEP 2 · Count electron domains on C
Carbon has 2 bonding domains (the two double bonds count as one domain each) and 0 lone pairs. That gives a linear geometry with a bond angle of 180°.
STEP 3 · Compare electronegativities
O (3.44) is more electronegative than C (2.55). Each C=O bond has a dipole pointing toward O.
STEP 4 · Vector addition of dipoles
Because the molecule is linear and symmetric, the two C=O dipoles point in opposite directions and are equal in magnitude. They cancel out, giving a net dipole moment of zero.
ANSWER
(a) Linear, 180° bond angle.
(b) Each C=O bond is polar (O is more electronegative than C), but the two bond dipoles are equal in magnitude and point in opposite directions due to the linear geometry. They cancel, so the molecule has no net dipole.
You Do
SHORT-ANSWER FRQ

(a) Predict the molecular geometry of H₂O.
(b) The O–H bonds are polar. Explain whether the molecule is polar or nonpolar overall, and justify using geometry.

Sample Response
(a) Bent (or angular), with a bond angle of about 104.5°. Oxygen has 2 bonding domains and 2 lone pairs, giving a tetrahedral electron geometry but a bent molecular geometry.

(b) The molecule is polar. Each O–H bond is polar because O is more electronegative than H. Unlike CO₂, the bent shape means the two bond dipoles do not point in opposite directions — they add together to give a net dipole pointing from the H atoms toward the O.
TOPIC 03 · UNIT 3

Stoichiometry & Limiting Reactant

We Do
LONG-ANSWER FRQ

Consider the reaction: N₂(g) + 3 H₂(g) → 2 NH₃(g)

A reaction vessel contains 14.0 g of N₂ and 3.00 g of H₂.
(a) Identify the limiting reactant.
(b) Calculate the theoretical yield of NH₃ in grams.

STEP 1 · Convert grams to moles
mol N₂ = 14.0 g ÷ 28.02 g/mol = 0.500 mol mol H₂ = 3.00 g ÷ 2.02 g/mol = 1.49 mol
STEP 2 · Determine the limiting reactant
From the equation, 1 mol N₂ needs 3 mol H₂. So 0.500 mol N₂ would need 0.500 × 3 = 1.50 mol H₂. We only have 1.49 mol H₂, which is just barely short — so H₂ is the limiting reactant.
STEP 3 · Use limiting reactant to find mol NH₃
mol NH₃ = 1.49 mol H₂ × (2 mol NH₃ / 3 mol H₂) = 0.990 mol NH₃
STEP 4 · Convert mol NH₃ to grams
mass NH₃ = 0.990 mol × 17.04 g/mol = 16.9 g
ANSWER
(a) H₂ is the limiting reactant.
(b) Theoretical yield ≈ 16.9 g NH₃.
You Do
LONG-ANSWER FRQ

Consider the reaction: 2 Al(s) + 3 Cl₂(g) → 2 AlCl₃(s)

A reaction vessel contains 5.40 g of Al and 12.0 g of Cl₂.
(a) Identify the limiting reactant.
(b) Calculate the theoretical yield of AlCl₃ in grams.
Molar masses: Al = 26.98 g/mol, Cl₂ = 70.90 g/mol, AlCl₃ = 133.33 g/mol

Sample Response
Step 1 — Convert to moles: mol Al = 5.40 g ÷ 26.98 g/mol = 0.200 mol mol Cl₂ = 12.0 g ÷ 70.90 g/mol = 0.169 mol Step 2 — Find limiting reactant: 2 mol Al needs 3 mol Cl₂, so 0.200 mol Al would need 0.200 × (3/2) = 0.300 mol Cl₂. We only have 0.169 mol Cl₂, so Cl₂ is the limiting reactant.

Step 3 — Find mol AlCl₃: mol AlCl₃ = 0.169 mol Cl₂ × (2 mol AlCl₃ / 3 mol Cl₂) = 0.113 mol Step 4 — Convert to grams: mass AlCl₃ = 0.113 mol × 133.33 g/mol = 15.0 g (a) Cl₂ is the limiting reactant.
(b) Theoretical yield ≈ 15.0 g AlCl₃.
TOPIC 04 · UNIT 3

Gas Laws & Ideal Gas Equation

We Do
MULTIPLE CHOICE

A sample of O₂ gas occupies 2.50 L at 300 K and 1.00 atm. If the temperature is increased to 450 K at constant pressure, what is the new volume?

A.1.67 L
B.2.50 L
C.3.75 L
D.4.50 L
STEP 1 · Identify what's constant
Pressure is constant. Moles are constant (sealed sample). Temperature and volume change. This is Charles's Law: V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂.
STEP 2 · Verify temperatures are in Kelvin
Both 300 K and 450 K are already in Kelvin. Good — never plug Celsius into gas law equations.
STEP 3 · Solve for V₂
V₂ = V₁ × (T₂ / T₁) V₂ = 2.50 L × (450 K / 300 K) = 2.50 × 1.50 = 3.75 L
STEP 4 · Sanity check
Temperature increased by a factor of 1.5, so at constant pressure the volume should also increase by 1.5×. 2.50 × 1.5 = 3.75 L. ✓
ANSWER
C. 3.75 L

TOPIC 05 · UNIT 6

Thermochemistry & Calorimetry

We Do
SHORT-ANSWER FRQ

A 50.0 g sample of water at 22.0 °C is placed in a coffee-cup calorimeter. A 25.0 g piece of copper metal at 95.0 °C is dropped into the water. The final temperature of the system is 25.6 °C.

Calculate the specific heat capacity of copper. (Specific heat of water = 4.18 J/g·°C)

STEP 1 · Set up conservation of energy
In a calorimeter, heat lost by the hot object = heat gained by the cold object. −q_Cu = q_water, or equivalently q_water + q_Cu = 0.
STEP 2 · Calculate heat gained by water
Using q = mcΔT: q_water = (50.0 g)(4.18 J/g·°C)(25.6 − 22.0) q_water = (50.0)(4.18)(3.6) = +752 J
STEP 3 · Heat lost by copper
Heat lost by Cu equals heat gained by water in magnitude: q_Cu = −752 J ΔT for copper: 25.6 − 95.0 = −69.4 °C
STEP 4 · Solve for c of copper
c_Cu = q_Cu / (m × ΔT) c_Cu = (−752 J) / [(25.0 g)(−69.4 °C)] c_Cu = 0.433 J/g·°C
ANSWER
c_Cu ≈ 0.433 J/g·°C
(Accepted value is about 0.385 J/g·°C — the discrepancy in this problem reflects normal calorimeter heat loss.)
You Do
SHORT-ANSWER FRQ

A 75.0 g sample of water at 20.0 °C is placed in a coffee-cup calorimeter. A 40.0 g piece of an unknown metal at 100.0 °C is dropped into the water. The final temperature is 24.5 °C.

Calculate the specific heat capacity of the unknown metal. (c_water = 4.18 J/g·°C)

Sample Response
Step 1 — Set up: −q_metal = q_water

Step 2 — Heat gained by water: q_water = (75.0 g)(4.18 J/g·°C)(24.5 − 20.0) q_water = (75.0)(4.18)(4.5) = +1411 J Step 3 — Heat lost by metal: q_metal = −1411 J, with ΔT = 24.5 − 100.0 = −75.5 °C

Step 4 — Solve for c: c_metal = (−1411 J) / [(40.0 g)(−75.5 °C)] c_metal ≈ 0.467 J/g·°C This is close to the specific heat of iron (0.449 J/g·°C), so the unknown metal is likely iron.
TOPIC 06 · UNIT 7

Equilibrium & Le Châtelier's Principle

We Do
MULTIPLE CHOICE

Consider the equilibrium: N₂(g) + 3 H₂(g) ⇌ 2 NH₃(g), ΔH = −92 kJ/mol

Which change would shift the equilibrium to produce more NH₃?

A.Increasing the temperature
B.Increasing the total pressure on the system
C.Adding a noble gas at constant volume
D.Removing some N₂ from the system
STEP 1 · Count moles of gas on each side
Left side: 1 + 3 = 4 mol gas. Right side: 2 mol gas. The forward reaction decreases total moles of gas.
STEP 2 · Evaluate (A) increasing temperature
The reaction is exothermic (ΔH < 0). Treat heat as a product. Adding heat shifts equilibrium backward (toward reactants) — produces less NH₃. (A) is wrong.
STEP 3 · Evaluate (B) increasing pressure
Increasing pressure shifts equilibrium toward the side with fewer moles of gas. That's the product side (2 mol vs. 4 mol). So the equilibrium shifts forward → more NH₃. (B) is correct.
STEP 4 · Evaluate (C) and (D)
(C) Adding a noble gas at constant volume does not change the partial pressures of N₂, H₂, or NH₃ → no shift. (D) Removing N₂ shifts equilibrium backward (toward reactants) to replace it → less NH₃.
ANSWER
B. Increasing pressure shifts toward the side with fewer moles of gas (the products).

TOPIC 07 · UNIT 8

Acid-Base Chemistry & pH

We Do
LONG-ANSWER FRQ

Acetic acid (CH₃COOH) is a weak acid with Kₐ = 1.8 × 10⁻⁵.

(a) Write the equilibrium expression for the ionization of acetic acid in water.
(b) Calculate the pH of a 0.100 M solution of acetic acid.

STEP 1 · Write the ionization equation
CH₃COOH(aq) ⇌ CH₃COO⁻(aq) + H⁺(aq)
STEP 2 · Write the Kₐ expression
Kₐ = [CH₃COO⁻][H⁺] / [CH₃COOH] = 1.8 × 10⁻⁵
STEP 3 · Set up an ICE table
Let x = [H⁺] formed at equilibrium.
Initial: [CH₃COOH] = 0.100, [CH₃COO⁻] = 0, [H⁺] = 0
Change: −x, +x, +x
Equilibrium: 0.100 − x, x, x
STEP 4 · Solve with the small-x approximation
Because Kₐ is small, assume x ≪ 0.100, so 0.100 − x ≈ 0.100: 1.8 × 10⁻⁵ = x² / 0.100 x² = 1.8 × 10⁻⁶ x = [H⁺] = 1.34 × 10⁻³ M Check: 1.34 × 10⁻³ / 0.100 = 1.3% — under 5%, approximation valid. ✓
STEP 5 · Calculate pH
pH = −log(1.34 × 10⁻³) = 2.87
ANSWER
(a) Kₐ = [CH₃COO⁻][H⁺] / [CH₃COOH]
(b) pH ≈ 2.87
You Do
LONG-ANSWER FRQ

Formic acid (HCOOH) is a weak acid with Kₐ = 1.8 × 10⁻⁴.

(a) Write the equilibrium expression for the ionization of formic acid in water.
(b) Calculate the pH of a 0.250 M solution of formic acid.

Sample Response
(a) Ionization: HCOOH(aq) ⇌ HCOO⁻(aq) + H⁺(aq) Kₐ = [HCOO⁻][H⁺] / [HCOOH] = 1.8 × 10⁻⁴ (b) Step 1 — ICE table: Let x = [H⁺]. Initial [HCOOH] = 0.250, equilibrium [HCOOH] = 0.250 − x, [HCOO⁻] = x, [H⁺] = x.

Step 2 — Apply approximation (0.250 − x ≈ 0.250): 1.8 × 10⁻⁴ = x² / 0.250 x² = 4.5 × 10⁻⁵ x = [H⁺] = 6.71 × 10⁻³ M Check: 6.71 × 10⁻³ / 0.250 = 2.7% — under 5%, approximation valid. ✓

Step 3 — Calculate pH: pH = −log(6.71 × 10⁻³) = 2.17 (a) Kₐ = [HCOO⁻][H⁺] / [HCOOH]
(b) pH ≈ 2.17 (more acidic than the acetic acid example because Kₐ is 10× larger).
