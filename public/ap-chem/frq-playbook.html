The one rule

Every "justify," "explain," or "predict" is worth zero without the reasoning. A correct answer with no justification is the #1 way to lose points on Section II. Always write the "because" sentence.

Time budget

105 minutes split across 7 questions. The College Board pacing is real. Long questions get more than twice the time of shorts because they're worth 2.5× the points.

Mental Model · Order of attack

Skim all 7 in the first 60 seconds. Crush the easy shorts first to bank points and settle your nerves. Save the scariest long for last so you can dump your remaining time into it without the rest of the test hanging over you.

The four verbs that decide your score

Every part starts with a verb. The verb is the AP exam telling you exactly what evidence they will accept for that point. Miss the verb, miss the point.

VerbWhat the grader is checkingZero-point responseFull-point response
Calculate Correct numerical answer with units. Setup shown (setup often earns its own point even if arithmetic is wrong). "The molarity is 0.5." "M = 10.0 g ÷ (40.0 g/mol) ÷ 0.500 L = 0.500 M"
Explain / Justify The principle named AND connected to the specific scenario. The conclusion alone is never enough. "The boiling point is higher because of stronger IMFs." "H₂O has hydrogen bonding (H bonded to O) while H₂S does not. Hydrogen bonds require more energy to break than London dispersion forces, so H₂O has a higher boiling point."
Predict / Identify Direct answer first, then one sentence of justification. The prediction without the reason earns partial credit at best. "The equilibrium shifts to the right." "The equilibrium shifts to the right. Adding more reactant increases Q below K, so the system produces more products to restore Q = K."
Draw / Complete Use the structure they gave you. Add only what's missing. Label atoms and lone pairs explicitly — graders can't assume you meant something. Lewis structure missing lone pairs on terminal atoms. Every atom satisfies octet (or duet for H), lone pairs drawn as dots, formal charges shown if nonzero.
The most expensive mistake on Section II

Writing "because of stronger intermolecular forces" for an explain prompt. That restates what the question is asking you to prove. The grader needs you to identify which IMF, why it's stronger, and how that causes the observed property. One vague sentence = zero points, even if the direction is correct.

The five FRQ archetypes

Section II questions are built from a small set of recurring tasks. Recognize the archetype and you start writing immediately.

1. The lab / experimental design question

Procedure (calorimetry, titration, gravimetric, gas collection) → calculate something → critique a source of error. The error analysis is where students slip.

The key to error analysis: trace the error through the equation. The grader wants to see every link in the chain, not just the final conclusion. "The answer will be too small" earns nothing without the chain. Follow each variable in q = mcΔT to its outcome.

Attack plan · Calorimetry question
  1. Find mass of solution: total volume × density (don't forget to add the dissolved solid if given).
  2. Calculate qsoln = mcΔT. Use Tf − Ti.
  3. Flip the sign: qrxn = −qsoln. Solution warms up → reaction released heat → ΔH is negative.
  4. Find moles of reaction using the limiting reactant.
  5. ΔHrxn = qrxn / molrxn. Include units and sign.
  6. For error analysis: identify which variable in q = mcΔT is affected → trace to final ΔH. Write every step.

2. The equilibrium / Le Châtelier question

Almost always: K expression → calculate Q → compare Q to K → predict direction. Then a temperature or volume change.

The conceptual core: K is a constant at a given temperature. Q is the current state. The system always moves to make Q → K. Temperature is the only thing that changes K itself — pressure and concentration changes shift position but leave K unchanged.

Analogy · Q is the scoreboard, K is the final score

Q is the current state of the products-to-reactants ratio at this instant, like a game in progress. K is where that ratio has to land at equilibrium: the final score. If Q is below K, you need more products (shift forward). If Q is above K, you have too many products (shift reverse). Q chases K.

Attack plan · Equilibrium question
  1. Write the K expression: products over reactants, coefficients as exponents, solids/pure liquids omitted.
  2. Calculate Q using current (not equilibrium) concentrations.
  3. Compare Q to K: Q < K → shift forward. Q > K → shift reverse. Q = K → no shift.
  4. For a change (add reactant, change volume, change temperature): determine the new Q, then decide direction.
  5. For temperature: treat heat as product (exothermic) or reactant (endothermic). ↑T shifts away from heat → changes K.
  6. For volume: pressure increases when volume decreases → system shifts toward fewer moles of gas (Δngas matters).

For temperature on K: treat heat as a product (exothermic) or reactant (endothermic) and apply Le Châtelier.

Reaction typeHeat treated as↑ Temperature shiftsK does what?
Exothermic (ΔH < 0)ProductReverse (consumes heat)K decreases

3. The acid-base / titration question

The AP exam tests four types of acid-base calculations: strong acid/base (simple [H⁺] or [OH⁻]), weak acid/base (ICE table with Ka), buffer (Henderson–Hasselbalch), and titration. Learn which region of the curve uses which approach.

Analogy · The buffer zone is the elevator stuck between floors

Before half-eq, the curve barely budges as base is added. That's the buffer working. Adding small amounts of base just nudges the HA / A⁻ ratio, not the pH. Then around equivalence, the buffer runs out and the elevator drops sharply.

Where you are on the curveWhat's in solutionHow to calculate pH
Start (0 mL base added)Weak acid onlyICE table with Ka: x² / ([HA]₀ − x) = Ka
Buffer region (before half-eq)HA and A⁻ both presentHenderson–Hasselbalch: pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA])
Half-equivalence point[HA] = [A⁻]pH = pKa (H–H simplifies, log(1) = 0)
Equivalence pointOnly A⁻ (conjugate base)Hydrolysis of A⁻ with Kb; pH > 7 for weak acid titration
Past equivalenceExcess strong basepH from [OH⁻]excess directly
Attack plan · Titration question
  1. Identify where on the curve you are (see table above) — this tells you which equation to use.
  2. Find equivalence volume: moles of acid = moles of base → Veq = nacid / Mbase.
  3. Find pKa from the half-equivalence point (read pH off the curve at Veq/2).
  4. For curve sketches: match start pH, equivalence point location, and post-equivalence plateau. Same acid = same start. Same base concentration halved → equivalence at half the volume.

4. The bonding / structure / IMF question

The pipeline always runs in this order — and you can only skip steps if the question tells you to:

Lewis structure → VSEPR geometry → hybridization → polarity → IMFs → property prediction

Each step feeds the next. You can't determine hybridization without VSEPR (electron domain count). You can't determine polarity without geometry (dipoles might cancel). You can't determine IMFs without polarity. Students who jump straight to "IMFs" without the earlier steps lose the intermediate points.

Attack plan · Bonding/IMF question
  1. Lewis structure: count total valence electrons, place bonds, fill octets with lone pairs, minimize formal charges.
  2. Electron domains: count bonds + lone pairs on central atom → geometry and hybridization (2=sp, 3=sp², 4=sp³).
  3. Molecular geometry: shape is based only on atoms, not lone pairs (bent, pyramidal, etc.).
  4. Polarity: polar bonds + asymmetric geometry = polar molecule. Symmetric = nonpolar even with polar bonds (CO₂, CCl₄).
  5. Identify IMF: use the decision tree (ionic > H-bond > dipole-dipole > LDF).
  6. Property prediction: stronger IMF = higher BP, lower vapor pressure, higher viscosity, more soluble in polar solvents.

5. The thermodynamics / favorability question

Two distinct concepts students often confuse: thermodynamic favorability (ΔG° < 0 — will it happen?) and kinetic accessibility (does it happen fast enough?). A reaction can be thermodynamically favorable and still not happen at room temperature because of a large activation energy. The AP exam mostly tests the first concept, but it will sometimes ask you to distinguish the two.

ΔG° = ΔH° − TΔS°
Attack plan · Thermodynamics question
  1. Determine the sign of ΔH°: exothermic (bonds formed > bonds broken, products more stable) = negative. Look for combustion, forming oxides, acid-base neutralization.
  2. Determine the sign of ΔS°: count moles of gas on each side. More gas on product side = positive ΔS°. More ordered products (solid from gas) = negative ΔS°.
  3. Use the ΔG matrix to determine temperature dependence. If ΔH and ΔS have the same sign, favorability depends on T.
  4. For Hess's law: work backward from the target equation. Reverse or scale given equations so intermediates cancel.
  5. For calorimetry: qrxn = −qsoln, then divide by moles of limiting reactant to get ΔH.

What scores, what doesn't

Every point on the FRQ rubric is earned by a specific piece of evidence. The grader applies the rubric literally — they can't give credit for something you almost said.

✓ Scores
  • Naming the principle (Coulomb's law, Le Châtelier, collision theory) by name
  • Units on every calculated answer
  • Setup shown before plugging in
  • "Because" sentences on every justify
  • Particle-level language for entropy ("more microstates")
  • Using data given in the question (reference the numbers)
  • Explicit comparison: "X is greater than Y because…"
Doesn't score
  • Vague hand-waving ("because of IMFs" — which IMF?)
  • Restating the prompt as your justification
  • Hedging ("might," "could," "depending on")
  • Listing every IMF instead of identifying the relevant one
  • Missing units on a calculated answer
  • Lewis structures without lone pairs
  • "The entropy is higher" without saying why at the particle level

Side-by-side: zero points vs. full points

Prompt typeResponse that earns zeroResponse that earns full credit
Explain why NaCl has a higher melting point than I₂ "NaCl has stronger intermolecular forces." "NaCl is an ionic compound held together by electrostatic attraction between Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions, which requires much more energy to overcome than the London dispersion forces between I₂ molecules."
Justify that ΔS° is negative for P₄(s) + 5O₂(g) → P₄O₁₀(s) "The entropy decreases because the products are less disordered." "Five moles of O₂ gas (high entropy — many accessible microstates) are converted to a solid product. Gas particles have far greater positional freedom than solid particles, so removing 5 moles of gas drastically reduces the number of microstates available."
Predict whether a precipitate forms (Q and K given) "Yes, because there are too many ions." "Yes. Q = 2.07 × 10⁻¹¹ > Ksp = 5.61 × 10⁻¹². When Q > Ksp, the solution is supersaturated and Mg(OH)₂ precipitates until Q = Ksp."

Formulas to know cold

The reference sheet has the formulas. Knowing how to use them is on you. For each formula below, the concept note explains what the formula is actually saying — if you only memorize the symbols, you'll misuse them under pressure.

Stoichiometry & gases

n = m / M · PV = nRT · M₁V₁ = M₂V₂ · P_A = χ_A · P_total

R = 0.08206 L·atm·mol⁻¹·K⁻¹ (pressure/volume) or 8.314 J·mol⁻¹·K⁻¹ (energy). Pick R based on the units you need in the answer.

M₁V₁ = M₂V₂ works because moles don't change during dilution. If you're mixing two solutions, the total volume is V₁ + V₂ and you need to dilute each species separately before calculating anything.

Analogy · Kinetic energy and the football team

KE = ½mv². An offensive lineman (~300 lb) running a 4.50 second 40-yard dash has way more kinetic energy than a wide receiver (~190 lb) running a 4.40. The receiver is faster, but mass wins because it's not squared. Speed is. The lineman is heavier and the velocity term, while smaller, still gets squared. This is why mass dominates KE for similar speeds, and why a small temperature increase (which raises average speed) hits gas kinetics so hard.

Acid-base

Ka · Kb = Kw = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ · pH + pOH = 14
Henderson-Hasselbalch: pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA])

Ka · Kb = Kw tells you that every weak acid has a conjugate base, and the stronger the acid (large Ka), the weaker its conjugate base (small Kb). This is how you calculate Kb of A⁻ from Ka of HA.

Mental Model · pH and pKa shortcuts

If pH = pKa → [HA] = [A⁻] (the half-eq sweet spot, log(1) = 0). If pH < pKa → more acid than conjugate base (acid form dominates). If pH > pKa → more conjugate base than acid. Read a titration curve like a thermometer: your position relative to pKa tells you the ratio of species in solution right now.

Equilibrium

For aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD: Kc = [C]ᶜ[D]ᵈ / [A]ᵃ[B]ᵇ
Kp = Kc(RT)^Δn · Ksp for solids: only products in expression

Solids and pure liquids never appear in K. Their "concentration" is defined as 1 by convention. Only gases (in Kp) and aqueous species (in Kc) are included. For Ksp, the denominator is always 1 because the solid is excluded — so Ksp = product of ion concentrations raised to their coefficients.

A large K (>>1) means products strongly favored at equilibrium. A small K (<<1) means reactants dominate. K = 1 means roughly equal concentrations of both.

Thermodynamics

q = mcΔT · ΔH° = Σ ΔH°f(prod) − Σ ΔH°f(react)
ΔG° = ΔH° − TΔS° · ΔG° = −RT ln K · ΔG° = −nFE°cell

ΔG° = −RT ln K is the bridge between thermodynamics and equilibrium. If ΔG° < 0, then ln K > 0, so K > 1 (products favored). If ΔG° > 0, K < 1. The more negative ΔG°, the larger K.

Analogy · Hess's law is a road trip

The total enthalpy change of a reaction is path-independent, like a road trip from Miami to Orlando. Direct on I-95, or via Tampa, or via Jacksonville. The miles you drive change, but your net displacement (start → end) is the same. Add up the ΔH of any path that gets you from reactants to products, and you've got your answer.

q signs in calorimetry

qrxn = −qsurr. Solution heats up → qsoln > 0 → qrxn < 0 → reaction is exothermic. When converting to molar ΔH, divide by moles of reaction, which often means identifying the limiting reactant first.

Kinetics

The reaction order is determined experimentally — you cannot read it from coefficients in the balanced equation (unless the question says it's an elementary step). The most common exam task: given a table of rate data, find orders by comparing trials where one concentration doubles.

Analogy · Catalyst is a tunnel through the mountain

A catalyst lowers Ea by giving the reaction an alternate path, like drilling a tunnel through a mountain instead of climbing over. Start altitude and end altitude (ΔH) are unchanged. Catalyst is consumed in one mechanism step and regenerated in another, so it doesn't appear in the overall equation.

Electrochemistry

E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode (always reduction potentials, then subtract)
ΔG° = −nFE° · F = 96,485 C/mol e⁻ · q = It

Cathode = reduction = gains mass (in metal-plating cells). Anode = oxidation = loses mass. In a galvanic cell, the more negative E° half-reaction is reversed (oxidation) — that's your anode. To maximize voltage, maximize the E° gap between cathode and anode.

The sign connection: positive E°cell → negative ΔG° → spontaneous → K > 1. These three (E°, ΔG°, K) are three windows into the same underlying thermodynamics.

The three-way link to memorize

E°cell > 0  ↔  ΔG° < 0  ↔  K > 1  ↔  spontaneous / product-favored
E°cell < 0  ↔  ΔG° > 0  ↔  K < 1  ↔  nonspontaneous / reactant-favored

Particle-level language

The exam awards points for reasoning at the molecular or atomic level — not for restating the macroscopic observation. "The boiling point is higher" is a macroscopic statement. "More energy is required to overcome the stronger hydrogen bonds between molecules before they can escape into the gas phase" is particle-level. Only the second one earns the point.

Here's the difference: a vague answer names the end result. A particle-level answer names the mechanism — what is happening at the particle level that produces that result.

ConceptVague (won't score)Particle-level (scores)
Higher entropy "more disorder" "more microstates accessible; gas particles have greater positional and translational freedom than solid particles"
Stronger IMF → ↑ BP "stronger forces hold the molecules together" "more energy required to overcome [name the IMF] between molecules and separate them into the gas phase"
Coulomb's law in IMFs or ions "the attraction is stronger" "higher charge or smaller ionic radius → shorter distance between charges → Coulombic attraction is proportionally stronger (F ∝ q₁q₂/r²)"
↑ T → faster reaction rate "higher temperature speeds up the reaction" "more molecules have kinetic energy ≥ Ea; frequency of effective collisions increases"
↑ Concentration → faster rate "more reactant is present" "more particles per unit volume → more frequent collisions between reactant molecules"
Catalyst → faster rate "the catalyst lowers the energy barrier" "provides an alternative reaction pathway with lower Ea; more molecules have enough energy to react; catalyst is consumed and regenerated so it does not appear in the overall equation"
ΔS° is negative "there is less entropy in the products" "reactants include X moles of gas; products are solid/liquid. Gas particles have many more accessible microstates than solid/liquid particles, so converting them reduces total entropy."
Pattern to practice

For any explain/justify prompt, train yourself to complete this sentence: "Because at the particle level, [mechanism], which causes [observed result]." The mechanism is what earns the point — the observed result alone never does.

VSEPR & polarity

Mental Model · Polarity is a tug-of-war

A molecule is polar only if (1) it has polar bonds AND (2) the bond dipoles don't cancel by symmetry. CO₂ has very polar C=O bonds, but they pull in opposite directions and cancel: nonpolar. H₂O has the same kind of polar bonds, but the bent geometry means they don't cancel: polar. Symmetric shape, nonpolar. Asymmetric, polar.

IMF decision tree

Given two substances, which has the higher boiling point / vapor pressure / solubility? The answer always comes from comparing IMF strength. Stronger IMF = harder to separate particles = higher BP, lower vapor pressure, higher viscosity.

The key conceptual point: you must identify which IMF type is present, then explain why it's stronger or weaker for one molecule vs. the other. Walk through this tree in order — each step is a potential FRQ point.

Analogy · IMFs are like Velcro at different strengths

Ionic attraction is industrial-grade hook-and-loop. Hydrogen bonding is heavy-duty Velcro. Dipole-dipole is a standard strip. LDF is the cheap fuzzy stuff that still adds up if you use enough of it (which is why a big nonpolar molecule with tons of contact area can outperform a small polar one).

Why each IMF has the strength it does

IMF typeWhat causes itWhat makes it strongerTypical BP range
Ionic bonds Full charges on ions attract each other (Coulomb's law) Higher charge on ions; smaller ionic radius (ions closer together) > 500 °C
Hydrogen bonds H bonded to N, O, or F creates a partial positive charge that attracts a lone pair on another N, O, or F More H-bond donors/acceptors per molecule; more electronegative atom (F > O > N) 50–300 °C
Dipole-dipole Partial charges from polar bonds create permanent alignment between molecules Greater difference in electronegativity between bonded atoms; larger overall dipole moment −50–150 °C
London dispersion Instantaneous fluctuations in electron density create temporary dipoles that induce dipoles in neighbors More electrons (higher molar mass); more surface area (longer, less branched chain = more contact) Variable; depends on size
The LDF size trap

A common mistake: assuming a small polar molecule always beats a larger nonpolar one. I₂ (nonpolar, molar mass 254 g/mol) has a boiling point of 184 °C. HCl (polar, molar mass 36.5 g/mol) boils at −85 °C. I₂ wins because its massive electron cloud creates very strong LDF that dwarf the weak dipole-dipole forces in HCl. When the size difference is large, LDF wins even over dipole-dipole.

Test-day checklist

Before you write anything on Q1
  • Skim all 7 questions. Mark the easy shorts.
  • Note any question with multiple parts that depend on each other (follow-through credit).
For every calculation

Setup → numbers → answer → units. In that order. The setup earns its own point.

For every "explain" / "justify"

Name the principle, then connect it to the specific scenario.

For every "predict"

One-word answer first ("increase" / "decrease" / "yes" / "agree"), then the reason.

If you get stuck for > 90 seconds

Write what you do know, move on, come back. Partial credit on guessed-but-justified is more points than blank.

Last 5 minutes

Every part has something written. Check units. Check signs.
