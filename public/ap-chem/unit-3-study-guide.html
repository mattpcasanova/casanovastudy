Topic 1

Intermolecular Forces (IMFs)

IMFs are attractions between molecules, not within them. They're much weaker than covalent or ionic bonds, but they determine boiling point, melting point, viscosity, surface tension, and solubility.

The Hierarchy (weakest → strongest)

ForceFound InStrength
London Dispersion (LDF)ALL moleculesWeak (gets stronger with size)
Dipole–DipolePolar moleculesModerate
Hydrogen BondingH bonded to N, O, or FStrong
Ion–DipoleIon + polar molecule (e.g. salt in water)Very strong
LDF clarification: Every molecule has LDFs — even polar ones and H-bonders. We just emphasize the strongest force when comparing substances. Larger/heavier molecules have stronger LDFs because they have more electrons that can shift around.

Hydrogen Bonding — The Special Case

Requires H directly bonded to N, O, or F. Why these three? They're small AND highly electronegative, creating a very strong δ+ on the H. The "FON" rule.

  • H₂O has TWO H-bonds per molecule (high BP, surface tension, ice less dense than water)
  • NH₃ and HF also H-bond but less aggressively than H₂O
  • CH₄ does NOT H-bond — C isn't electronegative enough
Check Your Understanding
Why does H₂O (BP 100°C) boil so much higher than H₂S (BP −60°C), even though S is heavier than O?
H₂O has hydrogen bonding (H to highly electronegative O); H₂S only has weak dipole–dipole forces. Sulfur isn't electronegative enough to form true H-bonds. IMF strength beats molecular weight here.

Predicting IMFs from Structure

  1. Is the substance ionic? → ionic bonds dominate (technically not an IMF, but strongest)
  2. Does it have H–N, H–O, or H–F? → hydrogen bonding
  3. Is the molecule polar (asymmetric)? → dipole–dipole
  4. Otherwise → only LDFs
Topic 2

Solids, Liquids, Gases

Particle Behavior by Phase

PhaseParticle MotionIMFsShape/Volume
SolidVibrating in placeStrong (locked)Fixed shape & volume
LiquidSliding past each otherModerateFixed volume, takes container shape
GasRandom, rapid, mostly empty spaceEssentially zeroFills container completely

Types of Solids

  • Ionic — cations + anions in lattice (NaCl). High MP, brittle, conduct when dissolved/molten.
  • Molecular — discrete molecules held by IMFs (ice, sugar). Low MP, soft.
  • Metallic — sea of delocalized electrons. Conductive, malleable, ductile.
  • Network covalent — atoms bonded in 3D network (diamond, SiO₂). Extremely high MP, hard.

Alloys (relevant for Q9 on this test)

  • Substitutional — host atoms replaced by similar-sized atoms (brass: Cu + Zn; stainless: Fe + Cr)
  • Interstitial — small atoms wedged into gaps (steel: Fe + small C atoms)
Check Your Understanding
Stainless steel contains Fe, Cr, and C. Why is Cr substitutional but C interstitial?
Cr has roughly the same atomic radius as Fe, so it can swap into lattice positions. C is much smaller and fits into the empty spaces between Fe atoms instead.
Topic 3

Phase Changes & Heating Curves

The Key Insight

During a phase change, temperature stays constant. Added energy goes entirely into breaking IMFs, not raising kinetic energy. You'll see this as flat plateaus on a heating curve.

Anatomy of a Heating Curve

  • Sloped sections — substance warming within a single phase (q = mcΔT applies)
  • Flat plateau at melting point — heat of fusion (ΔHfus) — solid → liquid
  • Flat plateau at boiling point — heat of vaporization (ΔHvap) — liquid → gas
  • ΔHvap > ΔHfus — vaporization breaks all IMFs; melting only loosens them

Calorimetry Calculations

q = m · c · ΔT (warming/cooling within one phase) q = n · ΔH (during phase change) Where: q = heat (J) m = mass (g) c = specific heat (J/g·°C) ΔT = T_final − T_initial n = moles ΔH = molar enthalpy (J/mol)
Conservation of energy: in a closed calorimetry system, qlost by hot object = qgained by cold object. Use this to solve for unknown temperatures or specific heats.
Check Your Understanding
Boiling water at sea level never goes above 100°C, no matter how high you turn the burner. Why?
All added energy is being used to break hydrogen bonds between water molecules (vaporization), not to raise their kinetic energy. Temperature stays pinned until all the water has converted to gas.
Topic 4

Ideal Gas Law & Dalton's Law

The Ideal Gas Law

PV = nRT P = pressure (atm) V = volume (L) n = moles R = 0.0821 L·atm/(mol·K) T = temperature (K — ALWAYS KELVIN!)

Derived Relationships (hold others constant)

  • Boyle's Law: P ∝ 1/V at constant n, T (hyperbola)
  • Charles's Law: V ∝ T at constant n, P (linear through origin)
  • Avogadro: V ∝ n at constant P, T
  • Gay-Lussac: P ∝ T at constant n, V

Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures

P_total = P_A + P_B + P_C + ... P_A = X_A · P_total (X = mole fraction)

In a gas mixture, each gas contributes pressure proportional to its mole fraction. They don't interact (ideal gases).

Check Your Understanding
Three sealed flasks contain different gases at the same temperature. To compare their pressures, what single quantity do you need?
n/V (moles per liter). Since P = (n/V)RT and T is fixed, the gas with the highest n/V has the highest pressure. Identity of the gas doesn't matter for ideal behavior.

When Gases Deviate from Ideal

  • Low temperature — molecules move slower, IMFs matter more
  • High pressure — molecular volume becomes non-negligible
  • Real gases pull each other inward (real P is slightly LOWER than ideal predicts)
Topic 5

Kinetic Molecular Theory (KMT)

The Five Assumptions of an Ideal Gas

  1. Particles are in constant random motion
  2. Particle volume is negligible compared to container volume
  3. No IMFs between particles
  4. Collisions are perfectly elastic (no energy loss)
  5. Average KE depends ONLY on temperature

The Big Result: Same T → Same KE

KE_avg = (3/2) k_B T = (1/2) m v²_avg
At the same temperature, all gases have the same average kinetic energy. Since KE = ½mv², lighter molecules must move FASTER to match the KE of heavier ones.

Maxwell-Boltzmann Distributions

Speed distributions for a gas at a given temperature. Reading them:

  • Lighter gas → curve shifted RIGHT (faster speeds), broader/flatter
  • Heavier gas → curve shifted LEFT (slower), taller/narrower
  • Higher temperature (same gas) → curve shifts right, flattens out
Check Your Understanding
At 25°C, which moves faster on average — H₂ or O₂?
H₂. Same temperature means same average KE, so the lighter molecule (M = 2) moves faster than the heavier one (M = 32). Roughly 4× faster, since v ∝ 1/√M.
Topic 6

Solutions & "Like Dissolves Like"

The Golden Rule

Like dissolves like. Polar solvents dissolve polar/ionic solutes. Nonpolar solvents dissolve nonpolar solutes. The IMFs in the solvent must be similar to those of the solute to "swap out" without an energy penalty.

Common Categories

  • Polar/ionic: water, methanol, ethanol, ammonia, NaCl, HCl
  • Nonpolar: hexane, benzene, CCl₄, oils, fats, I₂, gasoline

Dissolving Mechanics (Ionic in Water)

  1. Water dipoles surround the ion (O end toward cations, H end toward anions)
  2. Ion-dipole forces overcome the ionic lattice energy
  3. Ions become solvated (hydrated) and disperse

Charge Density Predicts Solvation Strength

Charge density = charge ÷ ionic radius. Higher charge density → stronger ion–dipole attraction with water. So:

  • Mg²⁺ (small, +2) attracts water MORE strongly than Na⁺ (larger, +1)
  • F⁻ (small, −1) attracts water MORE strongly than I⁻ (large, −1)
Check Your Understanding
Which would be more soluble in water: ethanol (CH₃CH₂OH) or hexane (C₆H₁₄)?
Ethanol — its −OH group can hydrogen bond with water. Hexane is purely nonpolar with only LDFs and won't mix with water (think oil and water).
Topic 7

Spectroscopy — Light Probing Matter

Different regions of the electromagnetic spectrum probe different parts of an atom or molecule. Energy of the photon must match the energy gap being probed.

EM Regions and What They Probe

RegionEnergyProbes
Gamma / X-rayHighestCore electrons, nuclei
UV / VisibleHighValence electrons (electronic transitions)
Infrared (IR)MediumBond vibrations (stretching/bending)
MicrowaveLowerMolecular rotations
Radio (NMR)LowestNuclear spin flips

Photoelectron Spectroscopy (PES)

High-energy photons knock electrons out of an atom. The instrument measures their binding energy (BE = energy required to remove that electron).

  • Higher BE = electron held more tightly = closer to nucleus (inner shell)
  • Lower BE = electron held loosely = outer shell (valence)
  • Peak height ∝ number of electrons in that subshell
  • Number of peaks = number of occupied subshells
KE_ejected = E_photon − BE (Same photon energy → low BE means more leftover KE → faster ejected electron)
Reading PES like a pro: Read peaks right-to-left. Lowest BE on the right = outermost subshell. Tally electrons by peak heights → get the electron configuration → identify the element.

Infrared (IR) Spectroscopy

IR photons excite bond vibrations. Vibration frequency depends on bond stiffness and atom mass:

  • Stronger/stiffer bond → higher frequency
  • Lighter atoms → higher frequency
  • Different bond lengths produce different frequencies → IR distinguishes them

UV/Visible Spectroscopy & Beer-Lambert Law

A = ε · b · c A = absorbance (no units) ε = molar absorptivity (M⁻¹cm⁻¹) b = path length (cm) c = concentration (M)

Absorbance is directly proportional to concentration. This is how we measure unknown concentrations from a calibration curve.

Mass Spectrometry

  • Each peak = one isotope at its specific mass
  • Peak height (abundance %) = how common that isotope is
  • Average atomic mass = weighted average of all peaks (Σ mass × abundance)
  • Number of peaks = number of isotopes
Check Your Understanding
A PES shows 3 peaks with electron counts 2 : 2 : 5 (highest BE → lowest BE). Which element?
Total = 2 + 2 + 5 = 9 electrons → Fluorine. Configuration 1s² 2s² 2p⁵ matches: highest BE peak = 1s² (closest to nucleus), middle = 2s², lowest BE = 2p⁵ (outermost).
Check Your Understanding
Which spectroscopic technique would distinguish between a C–H bond and a C–D bond (deuterium)?
IR. Different atomic masses on the same bond produce different vibrational frequencies (heavier D = lower frequency). UV/Vis wouldn't see this because the electronic structure is essentially identical.
Exam Tips

Common Traps on Unit 3 Questions

1. Mixing up molecular weight and IMFs

For boiling/melting point comparisons, IMFs usually win over mass — but not always. If two substances have the same IMF type, the heavier one boils higher (more LDFs). H-bonding always wins over LDFs.

2. Forgetting "all molecules have LDFs"

Even H₂O has LDFs. We name the strongest force when characterizing a substance, but LDFs are universal.

3. Phase change temperatures

During a phase change, T stays CONSTANT even though energy is added. The graph plateaus. Don't get tricked into thinking temperature is rising.

4. Kelvin vs Celsius in gas laws

Always convert to Kelvin (K = °C + 273) before plugging into PV = nRT. Forgetting is a classic AP trap.

5. KMT: same T means same KE, NOT same speed

Light molecules move faster to compensate for low mass. Speed ∝ 1/√M.

6. PES peak heights ≠ binding energy

Height = number of electrons (e.g. 6 for a full 2p). Position on x-axis = binding energy. Don't confuse them.

7. "Like dissolves like" isn't perfect

Small polar molecules dissolve in nonpolar solvents sometimes; large nonpolar molecules can be amphiphilic. Use it as a starting framework, not a rigid rule.
