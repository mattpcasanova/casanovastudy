Question 1
Long FRQ · 2025 Q1 10 pts

Answer the following questions about magnesium.

A. An incomplete mass spectrum for magnesium shows the percent abundance of magnesium-24 as 79%. The percent abundances of the other two natural isotopes, magnesium-25 and magnesium-26, are approximately equal.

[mass spectrum: bar at 24 amu at 79%, 25 amu and 26 amu blank]

i. Complete the mass spectrum by drawing thick lines for the percent abundance of magnesium-25 and magnesium-26.

Solution

Total must sum to 100%: 100 − 79 = 21%, split approximately equally → each line at about 10–11%. Both bars drawn to the same height in that range.

P1 The abundance for the two lines should be between 10 and 11.

ii. Describe the difference in atomic structure that accounts for the difference in mass between magnesium-25 and magnesium-26.

Solution

Magnesium-26 has one more neutron than magnesium-25. Both have the same number of protons and electrons; the mass difference comes from neutron count.

P2 Mg-26 has one more neutron than Mg-25.

A student prepares a 1.85 × 10⁻³ M solution of Mg(NO₃)₂(aq) in beaker 1 and a 2.80 × 10⁻⁴ M solution of NaOH(aq) in beaker 2.

B. A sodium ion, Na⁺, in beaker 2 has a weaker attraction to water than the Mg²⁺ in beaker 1 does. Explain this phenomenon using Coulomb's law and each of the following.

i. The relative charge of the ions.

Solution

The charge on Na⁺ (+1) is less than the charge on Mg²⁺ (+2). By Coulomb's law, a smaller charge produces a weaker Coulombic attraction between Na⁺ and the partial negative charge on water's oxygen.

P3 The charge on Na⁺ is less than the charge on Mg²⁺. A smaller charge results in a weaker Coulombic attraction between Na⁺ and water.

ii. The relative radii of the ions.

Solution

Na⁺ is larger than Mg²⁺ (Na⁺ has one more electron shell). The greater distance between Na⁺ and water's oxygen reduces the Coulombic attraction, since Coulomb's force decreases as distance increases.

P4 Na⁺ is larger than Mg²⁺, so the distance between Na⁺ and the oxygen on water is greater. As distance increases, Coulombic attraction decreases.

C. Calculate the pH of the solution in beaker 2.

Solution

NaOH is a strong base, so [OH⁻] = 2.80 × 10⁻⁴ M.

pOH = −log(2.80 × 10⁻⁴) = 3.553
pH = 14 − pOH = 14 − 3.553 = 10.447
P5 Correct calculated value of pH ≈ 10.45.

D. A student combines 35.00 mL of 1.85 × 10⁻³ M Mg(NO₃)₂(aq) with 50.00 mL of 2.80 × 10⁻⁴ M NaOH(aq). Calculate [Mg²⁺] after the two solutions are combined but before any reaction takes place. (Assume volumes are additive.)

Solution

Dilution formula M₁V₁ = M₂V₂ where V₂ is the combined total volume:

[Mg²⁺] = (1.85 × 10⁻³ M)(0.03500 L) / (0.03500 L + 0.05000 L)
[Mg²⁺] = (6.475 × 10⁻⁵) / (0.08500 L) = 7.62 × 10⁻⁴ M
P6 Correct calculated value of [Mg²⁺] ≈ 7.62 × 10⁻⁴ M.

E. The dissolution of magnesium hydroxide:

Mg(OH)₂(s) ⇌ Mg²⁺(aq) + 2 OH⁻(aq) Ksp = 5.61 × 10⁻¹²

i. Write the Ksp expression.

Solution
Ksp = [Mg²⁺][OH⁻]²

(Solids are not included in the K expression.)

P7 Correct Ksp expression.

ii. With [OH⁻] = 1.65 × 10⁻⁴ M after mixing, calculate Q.

Solution
Q = [Mg²⁺][OH⁻]² = (7.62 × 10⁻⁴)(1.65 × 10⁻⁴)² = 2.07 × 10⁻¹¹
P8 Correct calculated value of Q ≈ 2.07 × 10⁻¹¹, consistent with parts D and E(i).

iii. Will a precipitate form? Justify.

Solution

Yes, a precipitate will form. Q (2.07 × 10⁻¹¹) > Ksp (5.61 × 10⁻¹²). When Q > Ksp, the system is supersaturated and the reverse reaction (precipitation) is favored until equilibrium is reached.

P9 Q > Ksp, so a precipitate will form.

F. When HNO₃ is added to a saturated Mg(OH)₂ solution with undissolved solid present, does the undissolved Mg(OH)₂ increase, decrease, or remain the same?

Solution

Decrease. H⁺ from HNO₃ reacts with OH⁻ in solution, decreasing [OH⁻]. This makes Q < Ksp, so more Mg(OH)₂(s) dissolves to re-establish equilibrium. Less undissolved solid remains.

P10 Decrease. H⁺ reacts with OH⁻, decreasing [OH⁻] and causing Q < Ksp. More Mg(OH)₂(s) dissolves until equilibrium is re-established.
Question 2
Long FRQ · 2024 Q1 10 pts

A student studies the reaction of lactic acid (C₃H₆O₃) with NaOH:

C₃H₆O₃(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaC₃H₅O₃(aq) + H₂O(l)

A. Circle the H atom in the lactic acid structure that most readily participates in the reaction with NaOH.

[Structural formula of lactic acid: CH₃–CH(OH)–COOH, all H atoms drawn explicitly]
Solution

The rightmost H, which is the carboxylic acid H (on −COOH). This is the acidic proton; NaOH deprotonates it. The hydroxyl H on the alcohol group is much less acidic and does not transfer to NaOH under these conditions.

P1 The rightmost (carboxylic acid) H is circled.

B. The student dissolves 10.22 g of NaOH (40.00 g/mol) in enough water to produce 500. mL of solution. Calculate the molarity of the NaOH solution.

Solution
M = (10.22 g × 1 mol / 40.00 g) / 0.500 L = 0.2555 mol / 0.500 L = 0.511 M
P2 Correct molarity ≈ 0.511 M.

The student titrates a 25.0 mL sample of lactic acid solution. The titration curve shows the equivalence point at approximately 16 mL of NaOH added.

C. Use the graph to determine the approximate pKa of lactic acid.

Solution

The equivalence point is at ~16 mL → half-equivalence is at ~8 mL. At the half-equivalence point, [HA] = [A⁻], so pH = pKa.

Read the pH at 8 mL on the curve: pKa ≈ 3.9 (acceptable range 3.7 – 4.0).

P3 pKa = 3.9 (acceptable range: 3.7 – 4.0).

D. A particle diagram shows more lactic acid (HA) molecules than lactate (A⁻) ions.

i. Mark X on the titration curve at a point matching this diagram.

Solution

If [HA] > [A⁻], we are before the half-equivalence point. X should be placed at a volume between 3 mL and 8 mL on the curve.

P4 X is at V ≥ 3 mL and V < 8 mL.

ii. Justify the placement.

Solution

More acid (HA) particles are present than conjugate base (A⁻) particles, meaning the titration is before the half-equivalence point, where the ratio [HA]:[A⁻] equals 1:1.

P5 More acid particles than conjugate base particles → before half-equivalence.

iii. Draw the titration curve if NaOH concentration is doubled.

Solution

Doubling the titrant concentration halves the volume to reach equivalence: new equivalence at 8 mL (instead of 16 mL). The starting pH and final pH should be similar to the original curve since acid and base strengths are unchanged. Only the volume axis differs.

P6 Curve shows equivalence point at 8 mL.
P7 Curve begins at similar pH, rises sharply at a different volume from 16 mL, and ends at similar pH.

E. 100.0 mL of 0.500 M lactic acid at 20.0 °C is combined with 100.0 mL of 0.500 M NaOH at 20.0 °C. Final T = 23.2 °C. Density = 1.00 g/mL, c = 4.2 J/(g·°C).

i. Calculate q (in J).

Solution
msoln = 200.0 mL × 1.00 g/mL = 200.0 g
qsoln = mcΔT = (200.0 g)(4.2 J/(g·°C))(23.2 − 20.0 °C) = 2700 J
P8 Correct calculated value, q ≈ 2700 J.

ii. Calculate ΔHrxn in kJ/molrxn.

Solution

Moles of reaction = (0.100 L)(0.500 mol/L) = 0.0500 mol (equal moles of acid and base; both are limiting).

qrxn = −qsoln = −2700 J = −2.7 kJ
ΔHrxn = −2.7 kJ / 0.0500 mol = −54 kJ/molrxn
P9 Correct ΔHrxn ≈ −54 kJ/molrxn with negative sign.

iii. If heat is lost to surroundings, will the experimental |ΔHrxn| be smaller than the actual value?

Solution

Agree. Heat lost to surroundings means less heat warms the solution → final T is lower → ΔT is smaller → qsoln is smaller in magnitude → |ΔHrxn| comes out smaller than its true value.

P10 Agree. Heat lost from system → lower final T → smaller ΔT → smaller qsoln → smaller |ΔH|.
Question 3
Long FRQ · 2025 Q3 10 pts

White phosphorus is composed of P₄ molecules with a tetrahedral structure. Each P atom is bonded to the other three P atoms by single bonds.

A. Complete the Lewis diagram for P₄ by drawing the nonbonding electrons.

[Incomplete Lewis structure of P₄: four P atoms in a tetrahedron, all bonds drawn, no lone pairs shown yet]
Solution

Each P has 5 valence electrons. With 3 single bonds, each P uses 3 valence electrons in bonds → 2 electrons remain as one lone pair per P atom. Total: 4 lone pairs in the structure.

P1 One lone pair drawn on each of the four P atoms.

Equation 1: P₄(s) + 5 O₂(g) → P₄O₁₀(s). This reaction is thermodynamically favorable at 298 K.

B.i. The entropy change of the reaction, ΔS°, is negative. Using particle-level reasoning, explain why entropy decreases.

Solution

Gases have far more microstates (greater positional and energetic disorder) than solids. The reactants include 5 moles of gas; the products are all solid. Going from many gas particles to no gas particles drastically reduces the number of accessible microstates, so entropy decreases.

P2 Gas particles are more dispersed / have more microstates than solids; converting gaseous reactants to solid products decreases entropy.

B.ii. ΔH° is also negative. A student claims favorability is driven by enthalpy and not entropy. Is this claim correct?

Solution

Yes, the student is correct.

ΔG° = ΔH° − TΔS°

For the reaction to be favorable, ΔG° < 0. Given:

  • ΔH° < 0 (exothermic) → contributes a negative term to ΔG° → favors spontaneity (enthalpy-driven).
  • ΔS° < 0 → −TΔS° is positive → contributes a positive term to ΔG° → opposes spontaneity.

Therefore enthalpy is what makes the reaction favorable; entropy opposes favorability.

P3 Yes. ΔG° = ΔH° − TΔS°. ΔH° < 0 contributes to favorability; ΔS° < 0 means entropy does not contribute to favorability.

Equation 2: P₄O₁₀(s) + 6 H₂O(l) → 4 H₃PO₄(aq) (exothermic). Calorimetry data: 0.100 g P₄O₁₀, 100.0 g H₂O, Ti = 22.00 °C, Tf = 22.38 °C, M(P₄O₁₀) = 283.9 g/mol, c(H₂O) = 4.18 J/(g·°C).

C.i. Calculate q released in kJ.

Solution
msoln ≈ 100.0 g + 0.100 g = 100.1 g
q = mcΔT = (100.1 g)(4.18 J/g·°C)(22.38 − 22.00 °C) = 159 J ≈ 0.16 kJ
P4 Correct calculated value ≈ 0.16 kJ, with correct sig figs.

C.ii. Calculate ΔH°rxn in kJ/molrxn.

Solution

qrxn = −qsurr = −0.16 kJ. Moles of reaction based on P₄O₁₀:

molrxn = 0.100 g × (1 mol / 283.9 g) = 3.52 × 10⁻⁴ molrxn
ΔH°rxn = −0.16 kJ / 3.52 × 10⁻⁴ molrxn = −450 kJ/molrxn
P5 Correct calculated value consistent with C(i).
P6 Correct negative sign.

D. In a second trial, some P₄O₁₀ stuck to the weighing paper (P₄O₁₀ is limiting). Is ΔT less than, greater than, or equal to trial 1?

Solution

Less than. Less P₄O₁₀ reacts → less heat transferred to water → smaller temperature increase. The mass of water (100.0 g) and the specific heat are unchanged.

P7 Less than. Less P₄O₁₀ → less thermal energy released → smaller ΔT.

Equation 3: P₄(s) + 6 Cl₂(g) → 4 PCl₃(g), ΔH°rxn1 = −1148 kJ/molrxn
Equation 4: PCl₃(g) + Cl₂(g) ⇌ PCl₅(g), ΔH°rxn2 = −88 kJ/molrxn

E. Calculate ΔH°f for PCl₅(g) per equation 5: ¼ P₄(s) + 5⁄2 Cl₂(g) → PCl₅(g).

Solution

Apply Hess's law. Multiply equation 3 by ¼ to get one mole of PCl₃; then add equation 4:

  • (¼) × Eq 3: ¼ P₄(s) + 3/2 Cl₂(g) → PCl₃(g), ΔH = ¼(−1148) = −287 kJ
  • Eq 4: PCl₃(g) + Cl₂(g) → PCl₅(g), ΔH = −88 kJ
  • Sum: ¼ P₄(s) + 5⁄2 Cl₂(g) → PCl₅(g), ΔH°f = −287 + (−88) = −375 kJ/mol
P8 ΔH°f = ¼(−1148) + (−88) = −375 kJ/mol.

Particle-level diagram at 546 K for equation 4: 6 PCl₃, 2 Cl₂, 4 PCl₅. Each particle = 1.00 atm.

F.i. Calculate Kp.

Solution
Kp = P(PCl₅) / [P(PCl₃) · P(Cl₂)] = 4.00 / [(6.00)(2.00)] = 4.00 / 12.0 = 0.333
P9 Kp = 4 / (6 × 2) = 1/3 ≈ 0.333.

F.ii. Does Kp increase, decrease, or stay the same when T increases to 596 K?

Solution

Kp decreases. Equation 4 is exothermic (ΔH°rxn2 = −88 kJ/molrxn < 0). Treat heat as a product. Increasing T shifts equilibrium toward reactants by Le Châtelier, reducing the products-to-reactants ratio, so Kp decreases.

P10 Decrease. Exothermic reactions favor reactant formation at higher T, so Kp decreases.
Question 4
Short FRQ · 2025 Q4 4 pts

A scientist is investigating a mixture of CH₃OH (methanol) and H₂CO (formaldehyde).

A. Identify the hybridization of the valence orbitals of the C atom in H₂CO.

Solution

H₂CO has C double-bonded to O and single-bonded to two H atoms. 3 electron domains around C → sp² hybridization.

P1 sp².

B. Draw a single hydrogen bond between one CH₃OH and one H₂CO.

[Mixture diagram: CH₃OH and H₂CO molecules with explicit O–H and C=O bonds]
Solution

H-bond donors are H attached to N/O/F. CH₃OH has an O–H group (H is the donor). H₂CO has no N–H, O–H, or F–H, but its carbonyl O is an H-bond acceptor (lone pairs on highly electronegative O).

Draw a dashed line from the H of the −OH in CH₃OH to the O in H₂CO. (Donor → acceptor: O–H ⋯ O=C.)

P2 Dashed line between the O atom of one H₂CO molecule and the H atom of the −OH group of one CH₃OH molecule.

C. The scientist plans to cool the gaseous mixture to form a liquid mixture. Data:

SubstanceMelting pt (K)Boiling pt (K)ΔHvap (kJ/mol)
CH₃OH17633837.6
H₂CO18125424.2

i. Propose a temperature where both compounds are liquids.

Solution

Both substances are liquids between their respective melting and boiling points. T must be above both melting points (>181 K) and below both boiling points (<254 K).

Any temperature in the range 181 K to 254 K. A safe choice: 220 K.

P3 Proposed temperature in the range 181 K – 254 K.

ii. Calculate the thermal energy removed to condense 8.59 g of CH₃OH (32.04 g/mol) at its boiling point.

Solution

Condensation releases the same magnitude of energy as vaporization absorbs.

q = 8.59 g × (1 mol / 32.04 g) × (37.6 kJ / 1 mol) = 10.1 kJ
P4 Correct calculated value ≈ 10.1 kJ.
Question 5
Short FRQ · 2024 Q5 4 pts

Hydrogen gas and iodine gas react to form hydrogen iodide:

H₂(g) + I₂(g) ⇌ 2 HI(g) ΔH°rxn = −12.19 kJ/molrxn

A. Write the equilibrium constant expression Kc.

Solution
Kc = [HI]² / ([H₂][I₂])

All species are gases, so all appear in Kc. HI exponent is 2 from its coefficient.

P1 Correct Kc expression with exponent 2 on [HI].

B.i. A particle diagram shows 4 H₂ and 3 I₂ molecules, Q = 0.67. Draw the number of HI molecules needed.

Solution

Use particle counts as proxies for concentrations (same volume):

Q = (nHI)² / (nH₂ · nI₂) = (nHI)² / (4 × 3) = (nHI)² / 12 = 0.67
(nHI)² = 8.04 → nHI ≈ 2.8 → 3 HI molecules
P2 Correct drawing showing ~3 HI molecules.

B.ii. Hypothesize an experimental change that increases moles of HI over time (without adding HI).

Solution

To shift equilibrium toward more HI:

  • Decrease the temperature. The reaction is exothermic; lower T favors product formation and increases K.
  • Add more H₂(g) and/or I₂(g) to the rigid container. Increasing reactant concentration shifts equilibrium toward products by Le Châtelier.
P3 Decreased temperature OR added more H₂ and/or I₂.

B.iii. The mixture is transferred to a larger container at constant T. Do moles of HI change?

Solution

Remain the same.

For this reaction, Δngas = 2 − (1+1) = 0. Equal moles of gas on both sides means increasing volume decreases all concentrations by the same proportion, so Q = [HI]² / ([H₂][I₂]) is unchanged. Q still equals K, so no shift.

P4 Remain unchanged. Equal moles of gas on both sides → Q ratio unchanged → no shift.
Question 6
Short FRQ · 2025 Q6 4 pts

A galvanic cell. As the cell operates, the Zn(s) electrode gains mass and the Al(s) electrode loses mass.

Half-reactionE° (V)
Zn²⁺(aq) + 2 e⁻ → Zn(s)−0.76
Al³⁺(aq) + 3 e⁻ → Al(s)−1.66

A. Write the half-reaction for the oxidation that occurs at the anode.

Solution

Al loses mass → Al is oxidized. The anode is the site of oxidation.

Al(s) → Al³⁺(aq) + 3 e⁻
P1 Correct oxidation half-reaction.

B. Write the balanced net ionic equation for the overall reaction.

Solution

Multiply oxidation by 2 and reduction by 3 to balance electrons (LCM = 6):

  • Oxidation × 2: 2 Al(s) → 2 Al³⁺(aq) + 6 e⁻
  • Reduction × 3: 3 Zn²⁺(aq) + 6 e⁻ → 3 Zn(s)
2 Al(s) + 3 Zn²⁺(aq) → 2 Al³⁺(aq) + 3 Zn(s)
P2 Correct balanced net ionic equation.

C. Each electrode starts at 50.0 g. Which electrode's mass changes more? Justify with a calculation.

Solution

Zn experiences the greater mass change.

From the balanced equation, 2 mol Al lost corresponds to 3 mol Zn gained. Molar masses: Al = 26.98 g/mol, Zn = 65.38 g/mol.

ΔmAl per molrxn = 2 × 26.98 = 53.96 g (lost)
ΔmZn per molrxn = 3 × 65.38 = 196.1 g (gained)

For every 54 g of Al consumed, 196 g of Zn is deposited.

P3 Zn has the greater mass change, justified with correct stoichiometric calculation.

The standard Zn/Al cell has E°cell = 0.90 V. The scientist needs a galvanic cell with greater voltage and has access to these half-cells:

Reduction half-reactionE° (V)
Au³⁺(aq) + 3 e⁻ → Au(s)+1.50
Zn²⁺(aq) + 2 e⁻ → Zn(s)−0.76
Mn²⁺(aq) + 2 e⁻ → Mn(s)−1.19
Al³⁺(aq) + 3 e⁻ → Al(s)−1.66
Be²⁺(aq) + 2 e⁻ → Be(s)−1.85

D. Using the Zn half-cell paired with one of the other options, what is the MAXIMUM voltage at standard conditions?

Solution

To maximize E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode, pair Zn (−0.76 V) with the half-cell giving the largest gap.

Au³⁺/Au at +1.50 V (cathode), Zn anode1.50 − (−0.76) = 2.26 V
Zn cathode, Mn²⁺/Mn anode−0.76 − (−1.19) = 0.43 V
Zn cathode, Al³⁺/Al anode−0.76 − (−1.66) = 0.90 V
Zn cathode, Be²⁺/Be anode−0.76 − (−1.85) = 1.09 V

Maximum: Zn paired with Au³⁺/Au → E°cell = 2.26 V.

P4 E°cell = +1.50 − (−0.76) = +2.26 V.
