Question 1
Long FRQ · 2025 Q1 10 pts

Answer the following questions about magnesium.

A. An incomplete mass spectrum for magnesium shows the percent abundance of magnesium-24 as 79%. The percent abundances of the other two natural isotopes, magnesium-25 and magnesium-26, are approximately equal.

[mass spectrum: bar at 24 amu at 79%, 25 amu and 26 amu blank]

i. Complete the mass spectrum by drawing thick lines for the percent abundance of magnesium-25 and magnesium-26.

Solution

Every magnesium atom in nature must be one of the three isotopes (24, 25, or 26), so their percent abundances must sum to exactly 100%.

% abundance of Mg-25 + % abundance of Mg-26 = 100 − 79 = 21%

Because the two are "approximately equal," split evenly: each bar sits at about 10–11%. Draw both bars to the same height in that range.

P1 The abundance for the two lines should be between 10 and 11.

ii. Describe the difference in atomic structure that accounts for the difference in mass between magnesium-25 and magnesium-26.

Solution

Isotopes of the same element always have the same number of protons (otherwise they would be different elements) and the same number of electrons (in a neutral atom). The only structural difference between isotopes is their neutron count, which is what changes their mass number.

Mg-26 has one more neutron than Mg-25. Both have 12 protons and 12 electrons; Mg-25 has 13 neutrons and Mg-26 has 14 neutrons.

P2 Mg-26 has one more neutron than Mg-25.

A student prepares a 1.85 × 10⁻³ M solution of Mg(NO₃)₂(aq) in beaker 1 and a 2.80 × 10⁻⁴ M solution of NaOH(aq) in beaker 2.

B. A sodium ion, Na⁺, in beaker 2 has a weaker attraction to water than the Mg²⁺ in beaker 1 does. Explain this phenomenon using Coulomb's law and each of the following.

i. The relative charge of the ions.

Solution

Coulomb's law states that the force between two charges is proportional to the product of their magnitudes: F ∝ q₁q₂/r². The ion attracts water because the partial negative charge on water's oxygen (δ⁻) is attracted to the positive ion charge.

Na⁺ has a charge of +1; Mg²⁺ has a charge of +2. Substituting into Coulomb's law, the larger charge on Mg²⁺ produces a proportionally stronger attraction to water's oxygen — exactly twice as large if all else were equal. Na⁺'s smaller charge means a weaker ion–dipole attraction.

P3 The charge on Na⁺ is less than the charge on Mg²⁺. A smaller charge results in a weaker Coulombic attraction between Na⁺ and water.

ii. The relative radii of the ions.

Solution

Na⁺ and Mg²⁺ are isoelectronic — both have 10 electrons and 2 filled shells. However, Na⁺ has only 11 protons while Mg²⁺ has 12. More protons pulling on the same number of electrons means Mg²⁺ contracts its electron cloud more tightly. Therefore Na⁺ is larger than Mg²⁺.

From Coulomb's law, F ∝ 1/r². A larger ion means greater distance (r) between the ion's nucleus and water's oxygen → the denominator r² is larger → the force is weaker. Na⁺'s larger radius directly weakens its attraction to water.

P4 Na⁺ is larger than Mg²⁺, so the distance between Na⁺ and the oxygen on water is greater. As distance increases, Coulombic attraction decreases.

C. Calculate the pH of the solution in beaker 2.

Solution

Step 1 — Identify the species. NaOH is a strong base that dissociates completely, so [OH⁻] = [NaOH] = 2.80 × 10⁻⁴ M. We know [OH⁻] directly, not [H⁺], so we work through pOH first.

pOH = −log[OH⁻] = −log(2.80 × 10⁻⁴) = 3.553

Step 2 — Convert to pH using the relationship pH + pOH = 14 (valid at 25 °C):

pH = 14 − 3.553 = 10.447

A pH above 7 confirms this is a basic solution — that's a good sanity check.

P5 Correct calculated value of pH ≈ 10.45.

D. A student combines 35.00 mL of 1.85 × 10⁻³ M Mg(NO₃)₂(aq) with 50.00 mL of 2.80 × 10⁻⁴ M NaOH(aq). Calculate [Mg²⁺] after the two solutions are combined but before any reaction takes place. (Assume volumes are additive.)

Solution

The key insight is that moles of Mg²⁺ don't change when you mix the solutions — you're just spreading them over a larger volume. The question asks for the concentration before any reaction, so we only account for dilution.

Moles of Mg²⁺ = M₁ × V₁, and total volume after mixing = 35.00 mL + 50.00 mL = 85.00 mL:

moles Mg²⁺ = (1.85 × 10⁻³ M)(0.03500 L) = 6.475 × 10⁻⁵ mol
[Mg²⁺] = 6.475 × 10⁻⁵ mol / 0.08500 L = 7.62 × 10⁻⁴ M

Notice the concentration dropped — it was diluted by the added NaOH solution. This diluted value is what you'll use for Q in part E.

P6 Correct calculated value of [Mg²⁺] ≈ 7.62 × 10⁻⁴ M.

E. The dissolution of magnesium hydroxide:

Mg(OH)₂(s) ⇌ Mg²⁺(aq) + 2 OH⁻(aq) Ksp = 5.61 × 10⁻¹²

i. Write the Ksp expression.

Solution

To write any equilibrium expression: put products in the numerator and reactants in the denominator, using each stoichiometric coefficient as an exponent. Pure solids and pure liquids are omitted — their "concentration" is defined as 1 and doesn't appear.

Mg(OH)₂ is a solid → excluded. The products are Mg²⁺ (coefficient 1) and OH⁻ (coefficient 2):

Ksp = [Mg²⁺][OH⁻]²

The exponent of 2 on [OH⁻] is critical — students commonly forget it and lose this point.

P7 Correct Ksp expression.

ii. With [OH⁻] = 1.65 × 10⁻⁴ M after mixing, calculate Q.

Solution

Q (the reaction quotient) has the same mathematical form as Ksp, but uses the actual current concentrations in the mixed solution rather than equilibrium values. Plug in [Mg²⁺] from part D and the given [OH⁻]:

Q = [Mg²⁺][OH⁻]² = (7.62 × 10⁻⁴)(1.65 × 10⁻⁴)²
Q = (7.62 × 10⁻⁴)(2.7225 × 10⁻⁸) = 2.07 × 10⁻¹¹

Keep this value in mind — you'll compare it to Ksp in part (iii).

P8 Correct calculated value of Q ≈ 2.07 × 10⁻¹¹, consistent with parts D and E(i).

iii. Will a precipitate form? Justify.

Solution

Use the Q vs K decision framework:

  • Q < Ksp: solution is unsaturated → more solid dissolves (no precipitate)
  • Q = Ksp: system is at equilibrium → no change
  • Q > Ksp: solution is supersaturated → solid precipitates out ← this case

Q = 2.07 × 10⁻¹¹ is about 4× larger than Ksp = 5.61 × 10⁻¹². The solution contains more dissolved Mg²⁺ and OH⁻ than can coexist at equilibrium, so Mg(OH)₂ will precipitate until Q drops back down to Ksp.

P9 Q > Ksp, so a precipitate will form.

F. When HNO₃ is added to a saturated Mg(OH)₂ solution with undissolved solid present, does the undissolved Mg(OH)₂ increase, decrease, or remain the same?

Solution

Decrease. Trace the chain of reasoning step by step:

  1. HNO₃ is a strong acid; it fully dissociates and releases H⁺(aq).
  2. H⁺ reacts with OH⁻ in solution: H⁺ + OH⁻ → H₂O. This consumes OH⁻, so [OH⁻] decreases.
  3. With lower [OH⁻], Q = [Mg²⁺][OH⁻]² drops below Ksp.
  4. When Q < Ksp, the system shifts right — more Mg(OH)₂(s) dissolves to restore equilibrium.
  5. As the solid dissolves, the amount of undissolved Mg(OH)₂ decreases.

This is a classic Le Châtelier application: removing a product (OH⁻) by reacting it with acid drives the dissolution equilibrium forward.

P10 Decrease. H⁺ reacts with OH⁻, decreasing [OH⁻] and causing Q < Ksp. More Mg(OH)₂(s) dissolves until equilibrium is re-established.
Question 2
Long FRQ · 2024 Q1 10 pts

A student studies the reaction of lactic acid (C₃H₆O₃) with NaOH:

C₃H₆O₃(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaC₃H₅O₃(aq) + H₂O(l)

A. Circle the H atom in the lactic acid structure that most readily participates in the reaction with NaOH.

[Structural formula of lactic acid: CH₃–CH(OH)–COOH, all H atoms drawn explicitly]
Solution

NaOH is a strong base that removes the most acidic proton available. To find the most acidic H, ask: which H produces the most stable conjugate base when removed?

  • The −COOH (carboxylic acid) H: when removed, the resulting carboxylate (−COO⁻) is stabilized by resonance — the negative charge is delocalized over both oxygens. This makes it the most acidic H in the molecule.
  • The −OH (alcohol) H: when removed, the alkoxide (−O⁻) has no resonance stabilization — the charge stays on one oxygen. Much less acidic, and NaOH is not strong enough to deprotonate it here.

Circle the H on the −COOH group (rightmost in the structural formula).

P1 The rightmost (carboxylic acid) H is circled.

B. The student dissolves 10.22 g of NaOH (40.00 g/mol) in enough water to produce 500. mL of solution. Calculate the molarity of the NaOH solution.

Solution

Molarity = moles of solute ÷ liters of solution. Always convert grams → moles first using molar mass.

moles NaOH = 10.22 g × (1 mol / 40.00 g) = 0.2555 mol
M = 0.2555 mol / 0.500 L = 0.511 M
P2 Correct molarity ≈ 0.511 M.

The student titrates a 25.0 mL sample of lactic acid solution. The titration curve shows the equivalence point at approximately 16 mL of NaOH added.

C. Use the graph to determine the approximate pKa of lactic acid.

Solution

Why the half-equivalence point gives pKa: At the half-equivalence point, exactly half the acid has been converted to its conjugate base, so [HA] = [A⁻]. Plug this into the Henderson–Hasselbalch equation:

pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA]) = pKa + log(1) = pKa + 0 = pKa

Reading the graph: Equivalence point ≈ 16 mL → half-equivalence ≈ 8 mL. Find 8 mL on the x-axis and read the pH directly off the curve: pKa ≈ 3.9 (any value from 3.7–4.0 is accepted).

P3 pKa = 3.9 (acceptable range: 3.7 – 4.0).

D. A particle diagram shows more lactic acid (HA) molecules than lactate (A⁻) ions.

i. Mark X on the titration curve at a point matching this diagram.

Solution

Think about what the titration does chemically: each mole of NaOH added converts one mole of HA into A⁻. As long as more HA remains than A⁻, we haven't yet reached the half-equivalence point (where they'd be equal). So X belongs before the half-equivalence point, between 3 mL and 8 mL on the volume axis.

P4 X is at V ≥ 3 mL and V < 8 mL.

ii. Justify the placement.

Solution

The particle diagram shows more HA than A⁻, which means [HA] > [A⁻]. The half-equivalence point is defined as the volume at which exactly half the acid has been converted — at that point [HA] = [A⁻]. Since we still have more HA than A⁻, we have not yet reached the half-equivalence point. The X must be placed at a volume below the half-equivalence volume of 8 mL.

P5 More acid particles than conjugate base particles → before half-equivalence.

iii. Draw the titration curve if NaOH concentration is doubled.

Solution

The equivalence point occurs when moles of NaOH added = moles of acid present. If the NaOH concentration doubles, you need half the volume to deliver the same number of moles:

moles NaOH needed = unchanged → new Veq = 16 mL / 2 = 8 mL

What stays the same about the curve shape:

  • Starting pH — the lactic acid concentration and its pKa haven't changed, so the initial pH is the same.
  • The sharp rise at equivalence — still occurs, just at 8 mL instead of 16 mL.
  • Final pH (past equivalence) — the solution still contains excess NaOH and conjugate base at similar relative amounts, so the end pH is comparable.

The curve is essentially a horizontally compressed version of the original, shifted left with the equivalence point at 8 mL.

P6 Curve shows equivalence point at 8 mL.
P7 Curve begins at similar pH, rises sharply at a different volume from 16 mL, and ends at similar pH.

E. 100.0 mL of 0.500 M lactic acid at 20.0 °C is combined with 100.0 mL of 0.500 M NaOH at 20.0 °C. Final T = 23.2 °C. Density = 1.00 g/mL, c = 4.2 J/(g·°C).

i. Calculate q (in J).

Solution

In calorimetry, we measure how much heat the solution absorbed (qsoln = mcΔT), not the heat of the reaction directly. The mass of solution is the combined volume times the density:

msoln = (100.0 mL + 100.0 mL) × 1.00 g/mL = 200.0 g
ΔT = Tf − Ti = 23.2 − 20.0 = 3.2 °C
qsoln = mcΔT = (200.0 g)(4.2 J/(g·°C))(3.2 °C) = 2700 J
P8 Correct calculated value, q ≈ 2700 J.

ii. Calculate ΔHrxn in kJ/molrxn.

Solution

Sign convention: The solution gained heat (it warmed up), so qsoln = +2700 J. By conservation of energy, the reaction must have released that heat, so qrxn = −qsoln = −2700 J. A negative sign means exothermic — energy flowed from the reaction into the surroundings (the solution).

Finding moles of reaction: Both solutions are 0.500 M and 100.0 mL, giving equal moles of each reactant, so neither is in excess:

molrxn = (0.100 L)(0.500 mol/L) = 0.0500 mol

Converting to per-mole enthalpy:

ΔHrxn = qrxn / molrxn = −2.7 kJ / 0.0500 mol = −54 kJ/molrxn
P9 Correct ΔHrxn ≈ −54 kJ/molrxn with negative sign.

iii. If heat is lost to surroundings, will the experimental |ΔHrxn| be smaller than the actual value?

Solution

Agree — the experimental |ΔHrxn| will be smaller than the actual value.

This is a systematic error question. Trace the effect through the measurement chain:

  1. Some heat escapes to the surroundings instead of staying in the solution.
  2. Less heat in the solution → smaller temperature rise → smaller ΔT.
  3. Smaller ΔT → smaller qsoln = mcΔT.
  4. We calculate ΔH from qsoln, so a smaller q gives a smaller |ΔHrxn|.

This is a systematic error — it always causes the measurement to be an underestimate of the true value, not a random fluctuation.

P10 Agree. Heat lost from system → lower final T → smaller ΔT → smaller qsoln → smaller |ΔH|.
Question 3
Long FRQ · 2025 Q3 10 pts

White phosphorus is composed of P₄ molecules with a tetrahedral structure. Each P atom is bonded to the other three P atoms by single bonds.

A. Complete the Lewis diagram for P₄ by drawing the nonbonding electrons.

[Incomplete Lewis structure of P₄: four P atoms in a tetrahedron, all bonds drawn, no lone pairs shown yet]
Solution

Count valence electrons for one P atom, then figure out what's left after bonding:

  • P is in Group 15 → 5 valence electrons per atom.
  • Each P forms 3 single bonds (one to each of the other 3 P atoms), using 3 of its 5 valence electrons in those bonds.
  • Remaining electrons: 5 − 3 = 2 → one lone pair per P atom.

Draw one lone pair on each of the four P atoms. The overall structure has 4 lone pairs total. Each P obeys the octet rule: 3 bonds (6 electrons) + 1 lone pair (2 electrons) = 8 electrons around it.

P1 One lone pair drawn on each of the four P atoms.

Equation 1: P₄(s) + 5 O₂(g) → P₄O₁₀(s). This reaction is thermodynamically favorable at 298 K.

B.i. The entropy change of the reaction, ΔS°, is negative. Using particle-level reasoning, explain why entropy decreases.

Solution

Entropy measures the number of ways particles can be arranged and distributed — more microstates = higher entropy. The key particle-level comparison:

  • Gas particles are spread throughout the entire container volume, move freely in all directions, and have a huge number of possible positions and speeds — very high entropy.
  • Solid particles are locked into a fixed lattice with very little freedom of motion — very low entropy.

In this reaction, 5 moles of O₂(g) — a large amount of high-entropy gas — are consumed, and the only product is P₄O₁₀(s), a solid. The reaction converts high-entropy gas particles into a low-entropy solid, so the total entropy of the system decreases.

P2 Gas particles are more dispersed / have more microstates than solids; converting gaseous reactants to solid products decreases entropy.

B.ii. ΔH° is also negative. A student claims favorability is driven by enthalpy and not entropy. Is this claim correct?

Solution

Yes, the student is correct. Use ΔG° = ΔH° − TΔS° and evaluate each term's contribution to spontaneity (ΔG° < 0 = favorable):

ΔG° = ΔH° − TΔS°
  • ΔH° term: ΔH° < 0 → this term is negative → pushes ΔG° toward negative → favors spontaneity.
  • −TΔS° term: ΔS° < 0, so −TΔS° = −T(negative) = positive → pushes ΔG° toward positive → opposes spontaneity.

The two terms pull in opposite directions. Since the reaction is still favorable overall (ΔG° < 0), enthalpy must be large enough to overcome the unfavorable entropy contribution. Entropy is working against the reaction, not helping it — the student's claim is correct.

P3 Yes. ΔG° = ΔH° − TΔS°. ΔH° < 0 contributes to favorability; ΔS° < 0 means entropy does not contribute to favorability.

Equation 2: P₄O₁₀(s) + 6 H₂O(l) → 4 H₃PO₄(aq) (exothermic). Calorimetry data: 0.100 g P₄O₁₀, 100.0 g H₂O, Ti = 22.00 °C, Tf = 22.38 °C, M(P₄O₁₀) = 283.9 g/mol, c(H₂O) = 4.18 J/(g·°C).

C.i. Calculate q released in kJ.

Solution

Mass of solution: The P₄O₁₀ dissolves into the water, so the total solution mass includes both the solid and the water. Don't forget to add the solid mass — it's small but the problem provides it, so include it:

msoln = 100.0 g (water) + 0.100 g (P₄O₁₀) = 100.1 g
ΔT = 22.38 − 22.00 = 0.38 °C
q = mcΔT = (100.1 g)(4.18 J/g·°C)(0.38 °C) = 159 J ≈ 0.16 kJ

This is qsoln — the heat absorbed by the solution. Because the reaction is exothermic, the reaction released this heat into the solution.

P4 Correct calculated value ≈ 0.16 kJ, with correct sig figs.

C.ii. Calculate ΔH°rxn in kJ/molrxn.

Solution

Sign flip: The solution absorbed heat (qsoln = +0.16 kJ). By conservation of energy, the reaction released that same heat, so qrxn = −0.16 kJ. The negative sign indicates the reaction is exothermic.

Moles of reaction: P₄O₁₀ is the limiting reagent; one mole of P₄O₁₀ reacting = 1 molrxn.

molrxn = 0.100 g × (1 mol / 283.9 g) = 3.52 × 10⁻⁴ molrxn

Per-mole enthalpy:

ΔH°rxn = −0.16 kJ / 3.52 × 10⁻⁴ molrxn = −450 kJ/molrxn

Both the value and the negative sign are required for full credit.

P5 Correct calculated value consistent with C(i).
P6 Correct negative sign.

D. In a second trial, some P₄O₁₀ stuck to the weighing paper (P₄O₁₀ is limiting). Is ΔT less than, greater than, or equal to trial 1?

Solution

Less than. Less P₄O₁₀ reacts → less heat transferred to water → smaller temperature increase. The mass of water (100.0 g) and the specific heat are unchanged.

P7 Less than. Less P₄O₁₀ → less thermal energy released → smaller ΔT.

Equation 3: P₄(s) + 6 Cl₂(g) → 4 PCl₃(g), ΔH°rxn1 = −1148 kJ/molrxn
Equation 4: PCl₃(g) + Cl₂(g) ⇌ PCl₅(g), ΔH°rxn2 = −88 kJ/molrxn

E. Calculate ΔH°f for PCl₅(g) per equation 5: ¼ P₄(s) + 5⁄2 Cl₂(g) → PCl₅(g).

Solution

Hess's law strategy: ΔH°f is the enthalpy for making one mole of PCl₅ from its elements. We need to build that equation from the two given ones. Work backward from the target:

  • Target has ¼ P₄(s) on the left — Equation 3 starts with P₄(s), so use ¼ of it.
  • Target produces PCl₅(g) — Equation 4 produces PCl₅(g) directly. Add it as-is.
  • Check: PCl₃(g) appears as a product of scaled Eq 3 and as a reactant in Eq 4 — it cancels. ✓
  • Check Cl₂: Eq 3 (×¼) uses 3/2 Cl₂; Eq 4 uses 1 Cl₂ → total 5/2 Cl₂. ✓ Matches target.
(¼) × Eq 3: ¼ P₄(s) + 3/2 Cl₂(g) → PCl₃(g),   ΔH = ¼(−1148) = −287 kJ
Eq 4: PCl₃(g) + Cl₂(g) → PCl₅(g),   ΔH = −88 kJ
Sum: ¼ P₄(s) + 5⁄2 Cl₂(g) → PCl₅(g),   ΔH°f = −287 + (−88) = −375 kJ/mol
P8 ΔH°f = ¼(−1148) + (−88) = −375 kJ/mol.

Particle-level diagram at 546 K for equation 4: 6 PCl₃, 2 Cl₂, 4 PCl₅. Each particle = 1.00 atm.

F.i. Calculate Kp.

Solution

The problem states each particle represents 1.00 atm of partial pressure, so count particles to get pressures: P(PCl₅) = 4.00 atm, P(PCl₃) = 6.00 atm, P(Cl₂) = 2.00 atm.

Write Kp from equation 4 (PCl₃ + Cl₂ ⇌ PCl₅): products over reactants, with coefficients as exponents (all coefficients are 1 here):

Kp = P(PCl₅) / [P(PCl₃) · P(Cl₂)] = 4.00 / (6.00 × 2.00) = 4.00 / 12.0 = 0.333

Kp < 1 means at equilibrium, reactants are favored over products — this makes sense given PCl₅ readily decomposes at elevated temperatures.

P9 Kp = 4 / (6 × 2) = 1/3 ≈ 0.333.

F.ii. Does Kp increase, decrease, or stay the same when T increases to 596 K?

Solution

Kp decreases.

The key principle: temperature changes actually change the value of K (unlike pressure or concentration changes, which shift equilibrium but leave K the same). The direction depends on whether the reaction is exothermic or endothermic.

Equation 4 is exothermic (ΔH° = −88 kJ/mol). Think of heat as a product: PCl₃ + Cl₂ ⇌ PCl₅ + heat. Increasing temperature is like adding more "heat product" to the right side. By Le Châtelier, the system shifts left (toward reactants) to consume the added heat. The equilibrium shifts to produce less PCl₅ and more PCl₃ and Cl₂ → the numerator of Kp decreases while the denominator increases → Kp decreases.

P10 Decrease. Exothermic reactions favor reactant formation at higher T, so Kp decreases.

Short FRQ · 2025 Q4 4 pts

A scientist is investigating a mixture of CH₃OH (methanol) and H₂CO (formaldehyde).

A. Identify the hybridization of the valence orbitals of the C atom in H₂CO.

Solution

Hybridization is determined by counting electron domains around the central atom (each single bond, double bond, triple bond, or lone pair counts as one domain):

  • 2 domains → sp
  • 3 domains → sp²
  • 4 domains → sp³

In H₂CO, the C atom has: one C=O double bond (1 domain) + two C–H single bonds (2 domains) = 3 electron domains → sp² hybridization.

The unhybridized p orbital on C participates in the π bond of the C=O double bond, and the molecule is planar with ~120° bond angles.

P1 sp².

B. Draw a single hydrogen bond between one CH₃OH and one H₂CO.

[Mixture diagram: CH₃OH and H₂CO molecules with explicit O–H and C=O bonds]
Solution

A hydrogen bond requires both a donor (an H attached directly to N, O, or F) and an acceptor (a lone pair on an electronegative atom, typically N, O, or F).

  • CH₃OH: has an O–H bond → the H is the donor. ✓
  • H₂CO: the H atoms are attached to C (not N/O/F), so H₂CO cannot donate a hydrogen bond. However, the carbonyl oxygen has lone pairs → it can accept a hydrogen bond. ✓

A common mistake: assuming H₂CO can donate H-bonds because it has H atoms — it can't, because those H's are on carbon, not oxygen.

Draw a dashed line from the O–H hydrogen of CH₃OH pointing toward the carbonyl O of H₂CO. (O–H ⋯ O=C)

P2 Dashed line between the O atom of one H₂CO molecule and the H atom of the −OH group of one CH₃OH molecule.

C. The scientist plans to cool the gaseous mixture to form a liquid mixture. Data:

SubstanceMelting pt (K)Boiling pt (K)ΔHvap (kJ/mol)
CH₃OH17633837.6
H₂CO18125424.2

i. Propose a temperature where both compounds are liquids.

Solution

A substance is a liquid when T is above its melting point and below its boiling point. For both to be liquid simultaneously, T must satisfy both conditions at once — find the overlap:

  • CH₃OH is liquid between 176 K and 338 K.
  • H₂CO is liquid between 181 K and 254 K.
  • Overlap (both liquid): 181 K to 254 K — limited by H₂CO's lower boiling point and higher melting point.

Any temperature in that range is acceptable. A safe midpoint: 220 K. Note: you cannot propose a temperature where either substance would be a gas or a solid.

P3 Proposed temperature in the range 181 K – 254 K.

ii. Calculate the thermal energy removed to condense 8.59 g of CH₃OH (32.04 g/mol) at its boiling point.

Solution

ΔHvap is the heat absorbed when a liquid vaporizes. Condensation is the reverse process — the same magnitude of energy is released. The question asks for "thermal energy removed," which is the magnitude of heat released during condensation.

Convert grams to moles, then multiply by ΔHvap:

q = 8.59 g × (1 mol / 32.04 g) × (37.6 kJ/mol) = 0.2681 mol × 37.6 kJ/mol = 10.1 kJ

The answer is positive because the question asks for the magnitude of energy removed (heat flows out of the substance).

P4 Correct calculated value ≈ 10.1 kJ.
Question 5
Short FRQ · 2024 Q5 4 pts

Hydrogen gas and iodine gas react to form hydrogen iodide:

H₂(g) + I₂(g) ⇌ 2 HI(g) ΔH°rxn = −12.19 kJ/molrxn

A. Write the equilibrium constant expression Kc.

Solution

To write Kc: concentrations of products (raised to their coefficients) in the numerator, reactants in the denominator. Exclude pure solids and liquids — but all species here are gases, so all are included.

Kc = [HI]² / ([H₂][I₂])

The exponent of 2 on [HI] comes from its stoichiometric coefficient of 2 in the balanced equation — this is the most commonly missed detail on this type of question.

P1 Correct Kc expression with exponent 2 on [HI].

B.i. A particle diagram shows 4 H₂ and 3 I₂ molecules, Q = 0.67. Draw the number of HI molecules needed.

Solution

Since all particles are in the same fixed container volume, particle counts are proportional to molar concentrations. We can substitute particle numbers directly into the Q expression (they cancel in the ratio). Set Q = 0.67 and solve for the unknown number of HI particles:

Q = (nHI)² / (nH₂ · nI₂) = (nHI)² / (4 × 3) = 0.67
(nHI)² = 0.67 × 12 = 8.04 → nHI = √8.04 ≈ 2.8

Since we can only draw whole molecules, round to 3 HI molecules in the diagram.

P2 Correct drawing showing ~3 HI molecules.

B.ii. Hypothesize an experimental change that increases moles of HI over time (without adding HI).

Solution

Two categories of changes can shift equilibrium toward more products:

  • Decrease temperature: The reaction is exothermic (ΔH° = −12.19 kJ/mol). Treating heat as a product: H₂ + I₂ ⇌ 2 HI + heat. Lowering T removes "heat product," shifting equilibrium right. This also increases K itself — a temperature change is the only type of change that changes K.
  • Add more H₂ or I₂: Increasing reactant concentration makes Q < K. The system responds by shifting right (toward products) to restore Q = K. This increases the moles of HI present, though K itself stays the same.

Note: Adding a noble gas or increasing pressure does NOT shift this equilibrium (see part iii).

P3 Decreased temperature OR added more H₂ and/or I₂.

B.iii. The mixture is transferred to a larger container at constant T. Do moles of HI change?

Solution

Remain the same.

When volume increases, all concentrations decrease by the same factor. The question is whether this changes Q. Calculate Δngas (moles of gas on product side minus reactant side):

Δngas = 2 (HI) − 1 (H₂) − 1 (I₂) = 0

Because Δn = 0, the numerator and denominator of Q are both affected equally by the volume change. For example, if all concentrations drop by factor of 2:

Q = [HI/2]² / ([H₂/2][I₂/2]) = (½)²[HI]² / (½)²[H₂][I₂] = K (unchanged)

Since Q remains equal to K, there is no driving force to shift equilibrium. The moles of HI do not change. (This is unique to reactions where Δngas = 0.)

P4 Remain unchanged. Equal moles of gas on both sides → Q ratio unchanged → no shift.

A galvanic cell. As the cell operates, the Zn(s) electrode gains mass and the Al(s) electrode loses mass.

Half-reactionE° (V)
Zn²⁺(aq) + 2 e⁻ → Zn(s)−0.76
Al³⁺(aq) + 3 e⁻ → Al(s)−1.66

A. Write the half-reaction for the oxidation that occurs at the anode.

Solution

Identify the electrode from the physical observation: The Al electrode loses mass, which means Al atoms are leaving the electrode and entering solution as ions. Loss of mass = oxidation (loss of electrons). In a galvanic cell, the anode is always where oxidation occurs.

Reverse the standard reduction half-reaction to write the oxidation half-reaction:

Al(s) → Al³⁺(aq) + 3 e⁻
P1 Correct oxidation half-reaction.

B. Write the balanced net ionic equation for the overall reaction.

Solution

Electrons produced by oxidation must equal electrons consumed by reduction — they cannot appear in the final equation. Find the LCM of 3 (electrons from Al) and 2 (electrons consumed by Zn²⁺):

LCM(3, 2) = 6
  • Oxidation × 2:   2 Al(s) → 2 Al³⁺(aq) + 6 e⁻
  • Reduction × 3:   3 Zn²⁺(aq) + 6 e⁻ → 3 Zn(s)

Add the two half-reactions; the 6 e⁻ cancel:

2 Al(s) + 3 Zn²⁺(aq) → 2 Al³⁺(aq) + 3 Zn(s)
P2 Correct balanced net ionic equation.

C. Each electrode starts at 50.0 g. Which electrode's mass changes more? Justify with a calculation.

Solution

We need to compare the mass change per mole of reaction for each electrode. The stoichiometry tells us that for every 1 molrxn, 2 mol Al is consumed and 3 mol Zn is deposited:

ΔmAl lost per molrxn = 2 mol × 26.98 g/mol = 53.96 g
ΔmZn gained per molrxn = 3 mol × 65.38 g/mol = 196.1 g

For every mole of reaction, Zn gains 196 g while Al loses only 54 g. The Zn electrode experiences the greater mass change — both because more moles of it are involved (3 vs 2) and because each Zn atom is heavier than each Al atom.

P3 Zn has the greater mass change, justified with correct stoichiometric calculation.

D. Using the Zn half-cell paired with one of the other options, what is the MAXIMUM voltage at standard conditions?

Solution

Strategy: E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode. To maximize voltage, maximize the difference between the two half-cell potentials. With Zn fixed at −0.76 V, we can place it as either cathode or anode:

  • If Zn is the cathode, we want the most negative anode (largest |E°anode|) → Be²⁺/Be at −1.85 V.
  • If Zn is the anode, we want the most positive cathode → Au³⁺/Au at +1.50 V.

Zn cathode (−0.76 V), Be anode (−1.85 V)−0.76 − (−1.85) = 1.09 V
